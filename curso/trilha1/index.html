🎯 What is really selling

Break the idea that selling is pushing products and replace it with a problem-solving vision.

What is it:

Real selling means identifying a real customer pain point and offering a genuine solution. It’s not about convincing someone to buy something they don’t need — it’s about connecting need with solution.

Why learn:

It completely changes your posture. You stop being the "boring salesperson" and become someone the customer wants to listen to. This generates trust and recurrence.

Key concepts:

Selling as a service, problem solving, commercial empathy, relationships before transactions.

What is it:

Convincing presupposes resistance — you are trying to change someone's mind. Leading presupposes partnership — you guide the client to the best decision for them.

Why learn:

The salesperson who leads generates fewer objections, more trust and more natural closings. The client feels like they decided on their own.

Key concepts:

Driving vs persuasion, customer autonomy, consultative selling, guided decision making.

What is it:

When the customer perceives the value before knowing the price, the price is no longer a barrier. Value is the perception that it solves a problem that costs more than the investment.

Why learn:

Avoid the trap of competing on price. Those who present value before price sell more expensively and with less resistance.

Key concepts:

Perceived value, value anchoring, presentation sequence, price as a consequence.

What is it:

Just as a doctor does not prescribe without diagnosing, a professional salesperson does not offer a solution without understanding the problem. First you ask, then you present.

Why learn:

It eliminates the waste of talking about features the customer doesn't need. Your every word becomes relevant and targeted.

Key concepts:

Commercial diagnosis, active listening, sales as consultancy, personalized solution.

What is it:

Transactional sales are fast, focused on price and convenience. Consultative selling is based on relationships, diagnosis and tailored solutions. Each serves a context.

Why learn:

Knowing when to use each model avoids wasted effort. Trying to sell a commodity product consultatively is as wrong as selling a complex solution transactionally.

Key concepts:

Consultative selling, transactional selling, solution complexity, sales cycle, average ticket.

What is it:

The professional salesperson follows a replicable process, does not depend on charisma or luck. He prepares, diagnoses, presents value, handles objections and conducts the closing methodically.

Why learn:

Consistency beats isolated talent. A well-followed process generates predictable, scalable and teachable results.

Key concepts:

Professional mentality, sales process, commercial discipline, consistency, predictability.

🧠 The 5 levels of consumer awareness

Understanding where the customer is in the purchasing journey completely changes how you communicate.

What is it:

The consumer does not know they have a problem. He lives his routine without realizing that something could be better. There's no point in offering a solution — he doesn't even know he needs one.

Why learn:

Trying to sell to those who are at that level is like offering medicine to those who don't know they are sick. You need to educate first.

Key concepts:

Eugene Schwartz, awareness levels, market education, top-of-funnel content.

What is it:

The consumer feels the pain but does not know ways to resolve it. He knows something is wrong but doesn't know what to do. It's time to show that there are solutions.

Why learn:

This audience is receptive to educational content. If you are the one who presents the path, you will be remembered when he decides to act.

Key concepts:

Conscious pain, search for a solution, educational content, authority, lead nurturing.

What is it:

The consumer already knows that there are ways to solve their problem, but has not yet chosen which one. He is researching, comparing approaches and evaluating options.

Why learn:

Now is the time to differentiate your approach. Show why your method is superior, not just that it exists.

Key concepts:

Differentiation, value proposition, comparative, unique mechanism, social proof.

What is it:

The consumer already knows your offer and is comparing it with competitors. He wants to know why he should choose you, not someone else. It's time for proof, guarantee and clear differentiation.

Why learn:

At this level, what convinces are details: testimonials, guarantees, demonstrations. Generic speech no longer works.

Key concepts:

Social proof, guarantee, demonstration, case studies, specific objections, direct comparison.

What is it:

The consumer has already decided they want to buy. All you need is a final trigger — an offer, a deadline, a bonus. Anything that takes him out of inertia.

Why learn:

Many sellers lose sales at this level by not asking for closure. The customer is ready — just drive.

Key concepts:

Urgency trigger, scarcity, direct offer, clear CTA, natural closing.

What is it:

Using the same message for all levels is wasting effort. Each level requires different language, tone and argument. Aligning the message to the level is the secret to effective communication.

Why learn:

When you speak the right language to the right level, conversion skyrockets. Without this, you talk to everyone and no one listens.

Key concepts:

Segmentation by awareness, targeted communication, copy by level, content funnel.

💎 Building value before price

Master the art of making the customer perceive value before knowing how much it costs.

What is it:

Research shows that 7 out of 10 buyers accept paying more when they see clear and differentiated value. Price is only a barrier when value has not been built.

Why learn:

It shows that the problem is rarely the price — it's the perception of value. Changes the focus from “lowering price” to “increasing perceived value”.

Key concepts:

Price elasticity, perceived value, willingness to pay, premium pricing.

What is it:

If the perceived value is greater than the price, the sale happens. If the price seems greater than the value, the customer backs off. Every sale is this simple equation.

Why learn:

It allows you to diagnose why sales are not happening and correct the course — almost always the problem is insufficient value, not a high price.

Key concepts:

Value equation, perception vs reality, anchoring, price framing.

What is it:

Revealing the price too early kills the sale. Revealing too late creates distrust. The ideal moment is after the customer has already understood the value and is engaged in the solution.

Why learn:

Mastering price reveal timing is one of the most valuable skills in sales. Dramatically changes the conversion rate.

Key concepts:

Price timing, presentation sequence, value stack, gradual revelation.

What is it:

A practical sequence: identify the pain, amplify the cost of inaction, show the solution, prove it with results, only then reveal the price as an investment (not a cost).

Why learn:

Transforms any sales presentation. The customer starts to see the price as "cheap" compared to the problem it is solving.

Key concepts:

Value stack, pain amplification, investment vs cost, emotional ROI.

What is it:

Competing on price is a race to the bottom. There will always be someone cheaper. The only sustainable way out is to compete on value — to be the best solution, not the cheapest.

Why learn:

It frees the seller from the pressure to "lower price". When you compete on value, the margin increases and the customer is more satisfied.

Key concepts:

Commoditization, differentiation, margin, positioning, blue ocean vs red ocean.

What is it:

Real examples of how to build value in SaaS, services, retail, B2B and infoproducts. Each segment has particularities, but the principle is the same: value before price.

Why learn:

Seeing concrete examples makes it easier to apply in your context. Go from theory to practice in different market realities.

Key concepts:

Adaptation by segment, value cases, contextualized practice, benchmarking.

Module 1.4
1.4 ~60 min

❓ The art of asking

Master strategic questions that reveal the client's pain and desire.

What is it:

Offering without asking is how to give medicine without knowing the disease. Well-asked questions reveal the customer's real situation and allow us to offer the right solution.

Why learn:

Reduces objections because the offer fits perfectly. The customer feels that you understand their situation.

Key concepts:

Commercial diagnosis, active listening, open questions, discovery sequence.

What is it:

Four key questions: "What bothers you?", "How much does this cost you?", "What happens if nothing changes?", "What would be ideal?". Together, they reveal pain, cost, urgency and desire.

Why learn:

These are universal questions that work in any segment. Memorizing and practicing these 4 questions dramatically increases the quality of the sales conversation.

Key concepts:

Key questions, disclosure of pain, cost of inaction, desired future state.

What is it:

Implication questions that make the customer calculate the real cost of not solving the problem. “If this continues for another 6 months, what will be the impact?” The goal is to create real urgency.

Why learn:

Without urgency, the customer postpones. Amplifying questions create the emotional motivation to act now, not later.

Key concepts:

Implication questions, SPIN Selling, cost of inaction, real urgency, amplified pain.

What is it:

Questions that make the client visualize the ideal scenario. “If you could solve this, what would it be like?” Desire is the driving force behind the purchase — when the customer sees the desired future, they pay to get there.

Why learn:

People buy on emotion and justify it with logic. Making the customer feel the desire is more powerful than any rational argument.

Key concepts:

Future state, visualization, need-solution questions, positive motivation, aspiration.

What is it:

Neil Rackham's SPIN model: Situation (understand context), Problem (reveal pain), Implication (amplify consequences), Need for solution (create vision of the future). It is the most researched consultative sales framework.

Why learn:

SPIN and based on research with over 35,000 sales conversations. It's not opinion — it's science applied to sales.

Key concepts:

SPIN Selling, Neil Rackham, situation, problem, implication and need questions.

What is it:

Asking too much, asking without listening, excessively closed questions, interrogatory tone. The goal is conversation, not inquiry. Ask, listen, connect, then ask again.

Why learn:

Poorly asked questions generate distrust and close the customer. The difference between a good diagnosis and an interrogation is in the form, not the content.

Key concepts:

Errors in approach, tone of conversation, question-answer balance, rapport, naturalness.

🔄 Translate characteristics into impact

Stop listing features and start showing real results to the customer.

What is it:

Feature: what the product HAS. Benefit: what the product DOES. Impact: what this CHANGES in the customer’s life. Example: "5000mAh battery" → "Lasts all day" → "You'll never miss an important call again."

Why learn:

Most sellers stop at the feature. Those who achieve impact sell much more because they connect the product with the customer's real life.

Key concepts:

FAB (Features, Advantages, Benefits), value translation, impact communication.

What is it:

Use impact verbs: "This reduces your time...", "It speeds up the process of...", "It prevents you...", "It increases your...". Sentences that begin like this communicate immediate results.

Why learn:

Having a formula ready makes communication easier in the heat of the conversation. You will never be left without knowing how to present an appeal again.

Key concepts:

Impact verbs, translation formula, result language, value-oriented communication.

What is it:

Instead of “our software has 47 features,” say “our customers save 12 hours a week.” The result is what matters. Features are a means, not an end.

Why learn:

The customer does not buy features — they buy transformation. The faster you get to the result, the faster he sees value.

Key concepts:

Result vs resource, proof of value, impact metrics, result storytelling.

What is it:

Each market has its value language. For SaaS: “reduces churn”. For retail: "increases average ticket". For services: "reduces rework". Speaking the customer's language is essential.

Why learn:

Using generic jargon doesn't work. When you speak the specific language of the segment, the customer feels that you understand their world.

Key concepts:

Sector jargon, KPIs by segment, customer language, speech adaptation.

What is it:

Real examples: "We have 24-hour support" → "When a problem arises at 3 am, you won't be alone." "We have a dashboard" → "You can see in real time where you are losing money."

Why learn:

Seeing the translation in action trains your brain to do the same automatically. With practice, you never list features again — it always translates into impact.

Key concepts:

Practical translation, before and after, reframing, communication of results.

What is it:

Practical exercise: list 5 characteristics of your product/service and translate each one into benefit and impact. Use the formula: Feature → Benefit → Impact on the customer’s life.

Why learn:

Theory without practice does not change results. This exercise is the first step towards internalizing the translation of value as a habit.

Key concepts:

Deliberate practice, internalization, translation habit, immediate application.

Module 1.6

🛡️ Objections: what they are and why they exist

Understand objections as signs of interest and learn to welcome them with confidence.

What is it:

An objection is a doubt that prevents a decision. Those who are not interested simply leave. When someone objects, they are saying, "Convince me—I'm almost there."

Why learn:

Changes the seller's emotional reaction. Instead of fear, trust. Instead of defense, acceptance. The objection becomes an opportunity.

Key concepts:

Objection as a signal, implicit interest, opportunity for clarification, welcoming posture.

What is it:

95% of objections come down to 5 categories: "It's expensive", "I need to think about it", "I'll see later", "I want to compare", "It's not a priority". Mastering these 5 covers almost everything.

Why learn:

When you know there are only 5 real objections, you can prepare answers for each one. There is no longer any surprise — there is preparation.

Key concepts:

Universal objections, objection map, preparation, anticipation, response script.

What is it:

Objection is a specific barrier ("it's expensive"). Indecision and general paralysis ("I don't know"). The objection is answered with argument. Indecision can be resolved with simplification and security.

Why learn:

Treating indecision as an objection doesn’t work. You need to identify which of the two you are facing to use the right strategy.

Key concepts:

Diagnosis of objection, indecision, analysis paralysis, simplification, risk reduction.

What is it:

"It's expensive" is almost never about money. It's about fear: fear of making mistakes, of not working, of regretting it. The price is the shield — behind it is insecurity.

Why learn:

When you stop lowering prices and start addressing fear, conversion goes up dramatically. The right answer isn't discounting — it's security.

Key concepts:

Fear of making mistakes, perceived risk, guarantee, social proof, reduction of uncertainty.

What is it:

Three root causes: insufficient perceived value (didn't see a reason), fear of making mistakes (perceived risk), lack of urgency (no need now). Each cause calls for a different response.

Why learn:

Diagnosing the root cause avoids generic answers. Instead of a standard response, you address the real problem behind the objection.

Key concepts:

Root cause, objection diagnosis, insufficient value, perceived risk, lack of urgency.

What is it:

The protocol: 1) Welcome ("I understand perfectly"), 2) Understand ("tell me more about this"), 3) Respond with empathy and data. Never argue, never invalidate, never pressure.

Why learn:

The wrong stance turns objection into conflict. The right posture transforms objection into connection and trust.

Key concepts:

Welcoming, active listening, commercial empathy, AER (Welcome-Understand-Respond) protocol.

📊 The awareness funnel in practice

Apply levels of awareness in real content and communication strategy.

What is it:

In a cold public (who have never heard of you), the majority are unaware or aware of the problem. Few are ready to buy. This defines what type of content to prioritize.

Why learn:

Avoid the frustration of trying to sell to people who aren't ready yet. Adjusts expectations and communication strategy.

Key concepts:

Cold public, awareness distribution, top of the funnel, market education.

What is it:

A warm audience has already interacted with you — saw a post, watched a video, downloaded material. Distribution changes: more people are aware of the solution and the product, fewer are unaware.

Why learn:

Communication for a warm and different public. You can be more direct, more specific, more action-oriented.

Key concepts:

Warm audience, remarketing, nutrition, middle of the funnel, progressive engagement.

What is it:

Hot audiences already know your offer, already trust you and are considering buying. Most are aware of the product or very aware. And time for a direct offer.

Why learn:

Don't waste hot audiences on basic educational content. They want supply, proof and assurance — not more education.

Key concepts:

Hot audience, bottom of the funnel, direct conversation, offer, CTA, qualified lead.

What is it:

Top: unconscious and aware of the problem (educate). Middle: aware of the solution (differentiate). Background: product aware and very aware (convert). Each step has its role.

Why learn:

Organizes the entire content and sales strategy. You know exactly what to communicate at each stage of the funnel.

Key concepts:

ToFu, MoFu, BoFu, customer journey, content by stage, funnel strategy.

What is it:

The ideal content ratio: 60-80% education and free value, 20-40% offer and CTA. Those who only sell burn their audience. Those who only educate do not earn money. Balance and secret.

Why learn:

Defines the sustainable content strategy. You build authority and trust while monetizing naturally.

Key concepts:

Content/offer ratio, valuable content, authority, natural monetization.

What is it:

A single piece of content can speak to several levels at the same time. A post that educates (top), differentiates your approach (middle) and invites you to a conversation (bottom). The art is stacking levels.

Why learn:

Maximizes the return on each piece of content. You do less and achieve more — the essence of 80/20.

Key concepts:

Multi-level content, communication efficiency, value stacking, 80/20 applied.

Module 1.8

🧭 The salesperson as a driver, not as an actor

Consolidate the sales mentality as a process and prepare the transition to practice.

What is it:

The seller with a process closes on good and bad days. The processless seller depends on mood, the customer and luck. Consistency is what separates professionals from amateurs.

Why learn:

Predictability is the basis of any healthy business. Without process, you can't improve because you don't know what to measure.

Key concepts:

Sales process, consistency, predictability, continuous improvement, KPIs.

What is it:

A script is not to be read robotically. It's a structure that ensures you don't forget important points. Just as a musician rehearses the score and then improvises safely.

Why learn:

Eliminates the fear of "not knowing what to say". With a base script, you have the confidence to adapt and customize.

Key concepts:

Sales script, flexible structure, adaptation, personalization, trust.

What is it:

The salesperson-conductor knows where the conversation needs to go and guides the customer there with questions, pauses and intentional transitions. He doesn't react — he leads.

Why learn:

Conversations without direction don't end. The driver ensures that each step of the process happens in the right order, at the right pace.

Key concepts:

Conducting conversation, intentionality, sales flow, controlling the pace.

What is it:

The three pillars of professional sales: 1) Ask to understand, 2) Listen to capture what matters, 3) Explain value clearly and objectively. Master these three and sell anything.

Why learn:

Simplifies all the complexity of sales into three practical actions. It is the minimum framework for maximum results.

Key concepts:

Three pillars, simplicity, focus on the essential, basic commercial competence.

What is it:

The central thesis of the 8020 course: sales is a skill that is learned methodically, not a gift that is born with. Anyone can sell well if they follow the right process.

Why learn:

Eliminates the limiting belief of "I don't have a sales profile". If you have a method, you will have results. Point.

Key concepts:

Method vs talent, democratization of sales, structured learning, growth mindset.

What is it:

Track 1 built the conceptual foundation. Track 2 will transform these concepts into a practical method, with scripts, templates and exercises to apply immediately.

Why learn:

Connects theory with practice. You now have the mindset — on the next path, you will have the tools to act.

Key concepts:

Theory-practice transition, bridge to action, continuity of learning, next steps.
