1. Process vs improvisation

Why Consistency Beats Isolated Talent — Every Time

There is a dangerous myth in the world of sales: that the best salespeople are those with "natural talent", who improvise with charisma and close deals based on lip service. The reality is very different.The seller who relies on improvisation has random results. Some days are great, some days are terrible, and he never knows why.

The salesperson who follows a process — even if he is less charismatic — haspredictability. He knows what works, he knows what to measure, he knows what to correct. Results may start out more modest, but grow consistently because they are replicable.

❌ Impromptu seller

  • ▸Results fluctuate between extremes
  • ▸Don't know how to replicate what worked
  • ▸It depends on the mood and energy of the day
  • ▸You can't teach others to do the same
  • ▸When you lose motivation, numbers plummet

✅ Seller by process

  • ▸Consistent and predictable results
  • ▸Knows exactly what it does and why it works
  • ▸Operates well even on bad days
  • ▸Can train others to reach the same level
  • ▸Continuously improves based on data

💡 Analogy

Think of two restaurants. One depends on the genius chef who improvises incredible dishes — when he's missing, the restaurant suffers. The other has documented recipes, clear processes and any trained cook delivers the same standard. Which one scales? Which survives in the long term?

2. Script as a tool, not a prison

Free structure — does not bind

The most common objection when talking about "following a script" is:"But I don't want to look like a robot". This concern is valid, but it arises from a confusion betweenrigid script(read word for word) andstructural script(follow the structure, use your words).

The structural script is like a musical score. The musician who knows the score can improvise on it safely. Someone who doesn't know any sheet music improvises without direction — sometimes it sounds good, most of the time it sounds confusing.

🎵 Think like this

Jazz — the most "improvised" music there is — is played by musicians who master musical theory, scales and progressions. Improvisation only works because there is a structure underneath. Sales is the same: you improvise better when you have structure.

3. The driver's mentality

Guide the conversation with intention, without pressure

The salesperson does not "push" — heguide. Think of a GPS: it doesn't force you to follow the route, but it shows you the best path and recalculates if you deviate. The driver does the same thing in the sales conversation.

The difference between leading and pressing is subtle but fundamental. Lead and be clear about where the conversation needs to go and use questions to get there. Pressure and ignore what the customer says and force the sale.

Conductor vs Reactive vs Pressor

DRIVER — The ideal

"You mentioned that the main problem is X. If there was a way to solve X in Y days, would it make sense for you to explore that?"

REACTIVE — Common, but weak

"I understand... and do you want to know anything else? I can send you some material..."

PRESSER — Destructive

"Look, this offer is for today only. If you don't close now, you're going to lose. Seriously, I'm helping you."

The 4 principles of the driver

01

You know where you want to get

Each question and each sentence has a clear purpose in the journey of the conversation.

02

Listen before speaking

Adjusts the route based on what the customer reveals. Don't follow a blind script.

03

Never lose control

Even when the customer deviates, he reconnects the conversation naturally.

04

Respects the customer's time

If it's not the time, don't force it. Plant the seed and cultivate the relationship.

4. Ask well, listen well, explain value clearly

The 3 pillars of an effective salesperson

If you had to summarize sales into three skills, they would be these:ask well, listen well and explain value clearly. Everything else — closing techniques, mental triggers, negotiation — is built on these three pillars.

Pillar 1
1

Ask well

Good questions make the customer think. Bad questions make the customer defend themselves. The difference lies in the intention and formulation.

❌ Bad question

"Do you need a CRM?"

✅ Good question

"How do you monitor today whether leads are being served on time?"

Pillar 2
2

Listen well

Listening is not waiting for your turn to speak. And process what the customer said and use that information for the next question or value presentation.

Technique: Active listening with confirmation

"If I understand correctly, your main challenge today is [X], and this is impacting [Y]. Is that right?" — This confirmation shows that you listened AND organizes the customer's thinking.

Pillar 3
3

Explain value clearly

Value is not what you think it is worth. And what the customer perceives solves their problem. The explanation of value must always connect the product's capacity with the customer's specific pain.

❌Features

"Our system has a real-time dashboard."

✅ Connected Value

"You mentioned that it takes 2 days to know if a lead has been answered. With the dashboard, you see this in real time and act on the same day."

5. Sales doesn’t need talent, it needs method

The central thesis of the entire 8020 course

This is the most important idea of ​​the entire course:Sales is a trainable skill, not an innate talent. Talent can help someone get started faster, but it's the method that determines who gets good — and who stays good over time.

Sales performance research consistently shows that high-performing salespeople are not the most charismatic or extroverted. They are those who follow processes, train regularly and analyze their results with discipline.

Talent stagnates because there is nothing to optimize. The method grows because each iteration adds measurable improvement.

Why does method beat talent?

  • ▸Method and scalability— You can teach 50 people the same method. You can't teach 50 people to have charisma.
  • ▸Method and measurable— You can identify which step is failing and correct it. With talent, you just know that "it's not working."
  • ▸Method and resistant— It works even when you are tired, unmotivated or having a bad day. Talent fluctuates with emotions.
  • ▸Method and cumulative— Each improvement in the process adds to the previous ones. The result is exponential, not linear.

💡 Reflection

If sales were talent, the best salespeople would always be the same. But in reality, ordinary people who adopt smart methods outperform "natural talents" all the time. The question is not "do I have a talent for sales?" — and "am I willing to follow a method?"
