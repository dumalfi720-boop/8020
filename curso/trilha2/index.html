🗺️ The value roadmap in 5 steps

A structured path to guide any sales conversation from diagnosis to closing.

What is it:

The first step in any consultative sale is understanding the customer's current situation. Before saying anything about your product, you need to listen, ask questions and map out the real scenario.

Why learn:

Sellers who diagnose before proposing close up to 3x more. The customer feels that you care about them, not just about the sale.

Key concepts:

Active listening, open questions, scenario mapping, rapport, consultative selling.

What is it:

After understanding the situation, you delve deeper into the problem. Use targeted questions so that the client can verbalize the pain, frustration and negative impact of what they are experiencing.

Why learn:

When the client speaks their pain out loud, it becomes more real. This creates natural urgency, without you having to press.

Key concepts:

Deepening questions, verbalization of pain, SPIN selling, explicit vs implicit problem.

What is it:

Here you help the customer see what happens if they don't solve the problem. The cost of not acting: loss of money, time, customers, opportunities. And project the negative future.

Why learn:

People buy more to avoid pain than to gain pleasure. Showing the consequence creates the urgency that was missing for the decision.

Key concepts:

Cost of inaction, future projection, loss aversion, natural urgency, quantified consequence.

What is it:

Only now do you present your solution — and you do this by connecting each benefit directly to the problem the customer just verbalized. It's not a list of features, it's a bridge between pain and relief.

Why learn:

Presenting the solution too early is the number one mistake salespeople make. When you wait for the right moment, the offer sounds like a natural response, not a forced pitch.

Key concepts:

Benefit vs feature, pain-solution connection, presentation timing, personalized value proposition.

What is it:

The price only appears after the value has been constructed. When the customer already understands the problem, the consequence and the solution, the price seems fair — because they have a reference to how much it costs NOT to solve.

Why learn:

Talking about price too early is the number 1 cause of “it’s expensive”. When you build value beforehand, the customer compares the price with the pain, not with the pocket.

Key concepts:

Value anchoring, price vs value, price momentum, framing, implicit ROI.

What is it:

A complete example of a sales conversation following the 5 steps, from initial greeting to closing. You see how everything connects in a real and natural conversation.

Why learn:

Theory without example doesn't work. Seeing the roadmap in action shows how transitions happen and how each step flows into the next naturally.

Key concepts:

Conversation flow, natural transitions, practical demonstration, applicable model, roleplay.

❓ The 4 questions that change everything

Four strategic questions that reveal the pain, quantify the problem and pave the way for the sale.

What is it:

The first question opens the door to real pain. It's not "how can I help?" — and a question that makes the client reflect on what bothers them most at that specific moment.

Why learn:

The main pain and the key to the sale. When you find it, everything becomes easier — the urgency appears on its own and the solution presents itself naturally.

Key concepts:

Open question, main pain, emotional trigger, point of frustration, active listening.

What is it:

This question transforms an abstract complaint into a concrete number. When the customer calculates how much they lose, the problem stops being "annoying" and becomes "expensive".

Why learn:

Numbers create urgency. "I'm losing R$5,000 a month on this" is much more powerful than "this bothers me". Quantifying is the step that turns interest into action.

Key concepts:

Quantifying pain, hidden cost, ROI of the solution, making the problem tangible, numerical anchoring.

What is it:

This question projects the negative future. It makes the customer imagine 6 months, 1 year without solving the problem. The projection creates a scenario that he wants to avoid at all costs.

Why learn:

The fear of losing is stronger than the desire to win. When the client visualizes the future without change, they feel the need to act now.

Key concepts:

Future projection, aversion to loss, organic urgency, negative scenario, accumulated cost of inaction.

What is it:

This question turns the game around: the client describes the ideal solution. If your offer fits what he just described, the sale practically closes itself.

Why learn:

When the client designs the solution and you show that you have exactly that, there is no objection. He convinced himself. You just confirmed it.

Key concepts:

Self-convincing, ideal solution, alignment of expectations, offer fit, bridge question.

What is it:

The 4 questions are not an interrogation. They are natural transitions within a conversation. Here you learn to use connectors, listen to answers and flow from one question to another without seeming robotic.

Why learn:

The technique only works when it seems natural. If the client perceives that you are following a script, they lose trust. Naturalness and what separates amateurs from professionals.

Key concepts:

Conversational transitions, natural connectors, active listening, real-time adaptation, fluidity.

📝 Complete script for beginners

A word-by-word script for those starting out in sales. Ready to adapt to your business.

What is it:

The first 30 seconds define whether the customer will listen or hang up. Here you learn to open the conversation with genuine interest, without pitching, without “I have an incredible opportunity”.

Why learn:

The opening is the most critical moment. If you lose here, it doesn't matter how good the rest of the script is. First impression doesn't have a second chance.

Key concepts:

First impression, rapport, consultative opening, conversational hook, pattern breaking.

What is it:

The development phase is where you ask the right questions to understand the customer's situation. Use active listening, make mental notes and identify the most relevant pain points.

Why learn:

The quality of the diagnosis determines the quality of the sale. Shallow diagnosis = weak sale. In-depth diagnosis = natural and objection-free sale.

Key concepts:

Diagnostic questions, active listening, pain mapping, problem hierarchy, validation.

What is it:

The transition is the moment when you leave the diagnosis and enter the presentation of the solution. It needs to be smooth, natural — the customer should feel that you are building a response for them, not pushing a product.

Why learn:

An abrupt transition breaks the trust built. The client feels that everything was a trap to get to the pitch. The smooth transition keeps the relationship intact.

Key concepts:

Conversational bridge, diagnostic summary, permission request, smooth transition, congruence.

What is it:

Instead of listing features, you connect each aspect of your solution to a pain point that the customer mentioned. "You said you waste 2 hours a day on X. What we do is eliminate that because..."

Why learn:

Customers don’t buy features — they buy solutions to their pain points. Present value in the customer's language and what differentiates consultative selling from transactional selling.

Key concepts:

Value proposition, customer language, tangible benefit, cross-reference with diagnosis, result storytelling.

What is it:

Closing is not a magic phrase. It is the natural conclusion of a well-conducted conversation. You ask "does it make sense to you?" instead of "are you going to buy?". Yes comes as a consequence, not as a concession.

Why learn:

Many salespeople lose the sale at closing by pushing too hard or not asking for the sale. The balance lies in making the decision with confidence and respect.

Key concepts:

Consultative closing, confirmation question, clear next step, gradual commitment, timing of order.

What is it:

The complete script bringing together opening, development, transition, presentation and closing in a single document. Ready for you to copy, adapt to your product and start using immediately.

Why learn:

Having a template script eliminates the paralysis of “what to say?” You adapt, train and evolve based on something concrete, instead of inventing from scratch every time.

Key concepts:

Adaptable script, conversation model, sales playbook, personalization, practical framework.

🛡️ How to answer the 5 most common objections

Ready and strategic responses to the most common objections in sales.

What is it:

"It's expensive" almost never means the price is too high. It means that the customer did not see enough value to justify the investment. The answer is not to discount — and to rebuild the value.

Why learn:

This is the most common objection and the one that causes sellers to lower prices unnecessarily. Knowing how to respond without giving in preserves your margin and your authority.

Key concepts:

Value anchoring, comparison with the cost of inaction, investment reframe, discount as a last resort.

What is it:

"I need to think" usually means "I'm not convinced" or "I'm afraid to decide now." The answer is to validate and help the client think out loud, not pressure to decide on the spot.

Why learn:

When the client goes to "think", in 90% of cases he cools down and doesn't come back. But pressing makes it worse. The secret is to conduct the "thinking" right there, together, with the right questions.

Key concepts:

Validation of the objection, guided thinking, clarifying questions, organic urgency, commitment to follow-up.

What is it:

"I'll see later" is a polite way of escaping. The answer is to understand what remains to decide now and create a concrete next step — not a vague “later” that never arrives.

Why learn:

"After" is the sales cemetery. Without a concrete next step with a date and time, the lead goes cold. But with a specific commitment, the chance of conversion remains high.

Key concepts:

Concrete next step, scheduling, structured follow-up, incremental commitment, lead nurturing.

What is it:

When the customer wants to compare, the answer is not to say bad things about the competitor. And help the client define the right comparison criteria — criteria where you naturally stand out.

Why learn:

Whoever defines the comparison criteria controls the decision. Instead of competing on your competitor's turf, you shift the battlefield to where you are strongest.

Key concepts:

Decision criteria, strategic positioning, differentiation, competitive terrain, consultative comparison.

What is it:

"Not a priority" means that the customer does not feel enough urgency. The answer is to reconnect with the pain, show the cost of waiting and ask "when would be the right time?" to keep the door open.

Why learn:

Often the lack of priority is a misdiagnosis — you didn't find the pain strong enough. Knowing how to reactivate pain without being insistent is an essential skill.

Key concepts:

Reactivation of pain, cost of waiting, relative priority, sowing to reap, strategic follow-up.

🧠 Adapt the script by level of consciousness

Each client is at a different stage. Learn to adjust your script for each level of consciousness.

What is it:

The unconscious public doesn't know they have a problem. Trying to sell it to him is like offering medicine to someone who doesn't know they're sick. The script here is educational — it raises awareness first and foremost.

Why learn:

If you use the same script for all levels of consciousness, you lose most of your customers. Know how to adapt and multiply your conversion rate.

Key concepts:

Awareness scale, market education, awakening content, pre-sale, educational authority.

What is it:

This customer knows they have a problem, but they don't know how to solve it. The script focuses on validating the pain, delving into the consequences, and positioning your solution as the logical path.

Why learn:

This is the most abundant audience and where consultative selling shines. If you master this script, you already cover most of the market opportunities.

Key concepts:

Validation of pain, deepening, projected consequence, solution as a logical response, natural transition.

What is it:

This client already knows what type of solution they need, but has not yet decided with whom. The script focuses on differentiators, social proof and what makes your offer unique in relation to alternatives.

Why learn:

This client is comparing. If you don't differentiate yourself clearly, he will choose based on price. Clear differentiation is the antidote to price wars.

Key concepts:

Differentiation, unique value proposition, social proof, authority, competitive positioning.

What is it:

This customer already knows your product. The script focuses on concrete evidence: testimonials, results from other clients, guarantees and risk reduction. It is time to generate final trust.

Why learn:

This customer is almost ready to buy. A well-timed social proof or a well-placed guarantee could be all that was needed for the yes.

Key concepts:

Social proof, testimonials, guarantee, risk reduction, success story, demonstration.

What is it:

This customer already knows everything — they know the problem, the solution and your product. He just needs one final push: a clear offer, special conditions or simply the direct question "shall we get started?".

Why learn:

With this audience, too much conversation gets in the way. Being direct and respectful of their time is what generates the conversation. Less speech, more action.

Key concepts:

Direct offer, clear CTA, special condition, real urgency, simplicity in conversation.

🎭 Simulation and practical training

Structured methods for training sales in practice with realistic simulations and feedback.

What is it:

A 60-minute training format: 15 minutes of focused theory, 30 minutes of practical simulation and 15 minutes of correction and feedback. Ideal proportion between learning and practicing.

Why learn:

Most sales training is theory. The 15-30-15 formula ensures that you practice twice as much as you study, drastically accelerating learning.

Key concepts:

Active learning, theory-practice ratio, structured simulation, immediate feedback, rapid improvement cycle.

What is it:

A good simulation has a defined scenario, customer profile, anticipated objections and clear rules. It's not "pretend you're a customer" — it's a structured scenario with real context.

Why learn:

Bad simulation generates bad learning. When the scenario is realistic, the training transfers to real life. The closer to reality, the more effective.

Key concepts:

Structured scenario, client profile, simulation briefing, planned objections, controlled realism.

What is it:

Critical points of observation: tone of voice, pauses, active listening, script sequence, handling objections, transitions, body language and closing. An assessment checklist.

Why learn:

Without clear observation criteria, feedback is vague. With a checklist, each simulation generates concrete insights and specific improvement actions.

Key concepts:

Assessment checklist, critical points, structured observation, qualitative metrics, standard of excellence.

What is it:

Constructive feedback follows the formula: Strong Point + Improvement Point + Concrete Action. It is not vague criticism — it is specific observation with practical suggestions for improvement.

Why learn:

Bad feedback is demotivating. Well-given feedback accelerates evolution. How you correct is as important as what you correct.

Key concepts:

Feedback sandwich, specificity, corrective action, motivation, continuous development.

What is it:

Recording your simulations and real sales is the most powerful accelerator. When you watch yourself, you notice vices, long pauses, missed opportunities that you would never notice in the heat of the moment.

Why learn:

Athletes watch their games. Musicians listen to their recordings. Professional salespeople review your conversations. It is the fastest method of identifying and correcting faults.

Key concepts:

Self-assessment, sales recording, systematic review, identification of patterns, accelerated evolution.

📆 The 7-day training

A day-by-day plan to get from scratch and be selling with confidence in a week.

What is it:

The first day is dedicated to memorizing and practicing the method's key questions. The 4 questions that change everything, the diagnostic questions and the transition questions.

Why learn:

Questions are the number 1 weapon of the consultative salesperson. If you dominate the questions, you dominate the conversation. And the foundation on which everything is built.

Key concepts:

Key questions, memorization, oral practice, spaced repetition, foundation of the method.

What is it:

On the second day, you train the ability to identify the main pain and desire behind each conversation. Practice with simulated scenarios, identifying language patterns that reveal hidden pain.

Why learn:

Most clients don't mention the real pain in the first sentence. Train your sensitivity to capture signals and what transforms a common conversation into a sales opportunity.

Key concepts:

Reading signs, explicit vs implicit pain, desire behind the pain, active empathy, language patterns.

What is it:

On the third day, you take each feature of your product and translate it into tangible benefit for the customer. "There's 24-hour support" becomes "you'll never be stuck waiting for help".

Why learn:

Customers don't buy features — they buy the results they generate. Mastering this translation is what makes your pitch sound like a solution, not a technical sheet.

Key concepts:

Feature vs benefit, value translation, result language, tangibles, personalized value proposition.

What is it:

On the fourth day, you put everything together and practice the complete script: opening, diagnosis, transition, presentation and closing. The goal is to do the entire flow without consulting notes.

Why learn:

Practicing each part separately is important, but the magic is in the integration. The complete script shows whether you can maintain the sequence and naturalness from start to finish.

Key concepts:

Integration, complete flow, practice without notes, naturalness, consistency.

What is it:

On the fifth day, focus completely on objections. Someone throws random objections at you and you practice the responses until they become automatic. The goal is to never get stuck again when faced with “it’s expensive” or “I need to think about it”.

Why learn:

Objections are predictable. If you have trained, you respond with confidence. If you haven't trained, you stutter and lose the sale. Repetition is what creates automatism.

Key concepts:

Objections training, automatic response, confidence under pressure, controlled improvisation, resilience.

What is it:

On the sixth day, you record your simulations and review them. Watch, note points for improvement and redo. And polish day—adjusting tone, pauses, rhythm, and confidence before going into the real field.

Why learn:

Seeing yourself from the outside is the most powerful exercise in self-knowledge in sales. Vices that you would never notice become obvious when you watch the recording.

Key concepts:

Recording, self-analysis, polishing, fine-tuning, final preparation.

What is it:

The last day is the baptism of fire: a real service with supervision. Someone more experienced accompanies (live or recorded) and gives detailed feedback. You leave knowing exactly where you are and what to improve.

Why learn:

Simulation is essential, but nothing replaces real contact. With supervision, you have the confidence to make mistakes and learn without serious consequences. And the bridge between training and the field.

Key concepts:

Supervised service, post-service feedback, training-field transition, practical mentoring, real trust.
