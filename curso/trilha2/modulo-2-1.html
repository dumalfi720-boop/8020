🗺️ The value roadmap in 5 steps

Topic 1

🔍 Step 1: Diagnose the situation

The first step of any successful sale andunderstand before proposing. Most salespeople start by talking about the product. 8020 salespeople start by listening to the customer.

How to diagnose in practice

  • ✓ Ask open-ended questions: "Tell me how it works today..."
  • ✓ Listen without interrupting — make a mental note of pain points
  • ✓ Repeat what you heard to validate: "So you're saying that..."
  • ✓ Identify 2-3 main problems before moving forward

✓ Consultative Seller

  • "Tell me how it works today?"
  • "What bothers you most about this?"
  • "How long has it been like this?"
  • "What have you tried to resolve it?"

✗ Transactional Seller

  • "Our product does X, Y and Z"
  • "We have the best price on the market"
  • "Can I make you an offer?"
  • "When can I send the contract?"

💡 Practical Exercise

In your next conversation with anyone, practice asking 3 questions before giving any opinion. Notice how the person opens up more when you ask with genuine interest.

Topic 2
2

🎯 Step 2: Explore the problem

After understanding the general situation, you need todeepen the pain. The goal is to get the client to verbalize the problem in detail — because when they say the pain out loud, it becomes more real and urgent.

The deepening technique

1

Customer mentions superficial problem

"Sales have dropped a little"

2

You go deeper with questions

"How much did they fall? How quickly? What do you think caused it?"

3

Client reveals real pain

"In fact, they dropped 30% in 3 months. I'm losing my best customers to the competitor"

📊 Perspective

Research shows that salespeople who do5 or more in-depth questionsclose 74% more than those who ask 1-2 questions. The depth of the diagnosis is directly proportional to the conversion rate.

Topic 3
3

⚠️ Step 3: Show the consequence

Now that the client has verbalized the pain, you help them seethe cost of not resolving. People buy more to avoid losses than to make gains — this is the principle of loss aversion.

Phrases that project consequence

"If this continues for another 6 months, how much will you have lost in total?"

"And beyond the financial, how does this affect your team? Your sleep?"

"Have you calculated how many customers you are losing per month because of this?"

“What happens to the business if this problem escalates?”

Consequence mathematics

😰
Problem today
R$5k/month lost
← 12 months without resolution →
Accumulated cost
💸
Consequence
R$60k lost

💡 Rule of Thumb

Never invent consequences — help the client calculate theirs. When the number comes from him, there is no discussion.The consequence the client calculates is always more powerful than the one you present.

Topic 4
4

💡 Step 4: Present the solution

Only now — after diagnosing, exploring and showing consequences — do you present your solution. And it does thisconnecting each benefit directly to a pain that the customer mentioned. It's not a list of features. And a personalized response.

The presentation formula

😰
Customer pain
"You said..."
🔗
Connection
"And that's exactly why..."
✅
Benefit
"You'll make it..."

✓ Presentation with value

  • "You mentioned that you lose 2 hours a day on this. Our system automates this process, giving you those 2 hours back to focus on sales."

✗ Valueless presentation

  • "Our system has a dashboard, automatic reports, integration with 50 tools and 24-hour support."

📊 Why does this work?

When you connect your solution to the pain the customer just verbalized, theyfeels like you created that for him. The same generic offer becomes personalized just by the way you present it.

Topic 5
5

💰 Step 5: Speak the price

Price is the last thing you talk about. When the customer already understands the problem, the consequence and the solution,the price is compared to the cost of not solving— not with how much he has in his pocket.

Natural anchoring

Cost of not resolving:R$60,000/year in losses

Investment in the solution:R$5,000/year

Net savings:R$55,000/year (12x ROI)

How to say the price with confidence

  • ✓ Speak the price in a firm voice, without apologizing
  • ✓ Connect to the value: "Considering that you lose R$5k/month..."
  • ✓ Be silent after mentioning the price — let the customer process
  • ✓ Never offer a discount before asking

💡 Golden Rule

After saying the price,stay silent. The first person to speak after the price loses. Let the customer process. Most of the time, silence works in your favor.

Topic 6
6

🎬 The complete itinerary in practice

Let's see the 5 steps in action in a complete example conversation. Notice how each stepflows naturally to the nextwithout seeming like an interrogation or a forced pitch.

Example: Sale of consultancy for a restaurant

STEP 1 — DIAGNOSIS

Seller:"Tell me, how has the restaurant been in recent months?"

Customer:"Look, the weekdays are slow. The weekends are still there, but during the week it's dead."

STEP 2 — EXPLORE THE PROBLEM

Seller:"How much do you think you lose per week with these slow days? If you were to take the weekend movement to Tuesday, Wednesday, for example?"

Customer:"It makes about R$3,000 to R$4,000 a week. And about R$15,000 a month that I leave on the table."

STEP 3 — CONSEQUENCE

Seller:"R$15 thousand per month... in a year it's R$180 thousand. And during that time, the fixed costs — rent, employees — remain the same, right?"

Customer:"Exactly. The cost is the same whether you sell it or not."

STEP 4 — SOLUTION

Seller:"So you need a way to bring in people during the week without relying solely on word of mouth. That's exactly what we do — we create a local marketing strategy that attracts customers on slow days."

STEP 5 — PRICE

Seller:"The investment is R$2,000 per month. Considering that you lose R$15,000, we just need to bring in 15% of what's left to pay for it."

📊 What makes this example effective

Note that the seller never said "our service has X features". Helet the client calculate their own lossand then positioned the solution as a fraction of the cost of not solving it. The price of R$2,000 seems small compared to R$15,000/month of loss.
