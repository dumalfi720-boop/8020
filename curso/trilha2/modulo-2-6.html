1. The 15-30-15 formula

The ideal proportion between theory, practice and correction

Most sales training fails for one simple reason:are 100% theory. The manager explains for 2 hours, the team takes notes, and on Monday everything goes back to normal. Knowledge evaporates because it has never been practiced.

The 15-30-15 formula solves this with a simple, proven structure:15 minutes of theory, 30 minutes of practical simulation, 15 minutes of correction and feedback. In 1 hour, you train more than in 8 hours of lecture.

Formula visual
15

Theory

Present ONE concept, ONE technique, ONE script. Don't try to cover everything at once. Less and more.

30

Simulation

Practice in pairs or trios. One is the seller, another is the customer, and the third observes. Rotation every 10 min.

15

Correction

Specific feedback from the observer and the “client”. What worked? What can improve? What's the next step?

Why does this ratio work?

  • ▸15 minutes of theory is enough— The brain absorbs better in short blocks. After 20 minutes of lecture, retention drops drastically.
  • ▸Minimum 30 minutes of practice— Less than that and you don't have time to make mistakes, correct them and try again. Repetition is where skill is formed.
  • ▸15 minutes of correction is essential— Without feedback, the person makes mistakes and consolidates bad habits. Feedback is what transforms practice into improvement.

💡 Practical tip

No need for a special room, projector or elaborate material. A cell phone with a timer, 2-3 people and a clear theme are enough. You can do this during your lunch break, before work or at the end of the day.

2. How to set up a realistic simulation

Use real situations to really train

Bad simulation and when someone says "pretend I'm the customer" and they both start laughing because no one takes it seriously. Good simulation and when youuse a real situation— a service that happened yesterday, a lead that is in the pipeline now, an objection that arose last week.

The more real the scenario, the more useful the training. The idea is not to "play sales" — it's to rehearse the real sale that will happen tomorrow.

Scenario template

📋 Simulation scenario template

CONTEXT

Describe who the customer is, what segment, company size, and how they came to you (referral, advertisement, website, etc.).

Example: "Clothing store owner, 2 employees, revenue R$30k/month. Came to Instagram after seeing a post about inventory management."

MAIN PAIN

What problem does the customer feel? Be specific.

Example: "You lose sales because you never know what's in stock. It's happened that you promise a product and it doesn't have it."

LIKELY OBJECTION

What is the most expected objection in this scenario?

Example: "I think it's too expensive for a small store like mine."

SIMULATION OBJECTIVE

What specifically do you want to train in this round?

Example: "Train the transition between lifting pain and presenting value."

Rules for a good simulation

  • ▸The "client" must be difficult, not impossible— Don't make it too easy (otherwise you won't train), don't block everything (otherwise it frustrates you).
  • ▸Keep the character— If you are the customer, don't go out of your way to give a tip to the salesperson. Write it down and talk later.
  • ▸Limited time— Each round 7-10 minutes. Too long it becomes a conversation and loses focus.
  • ▸Rotate the papers— Everyone must be a seller, customer and observer. Each role teaches something different.
TOPIC 3
👀

3. What to observe during the simulation

Checklist for the observer to evaluate each aspect of the sale

The role of the observer is the most important in the simulation. He's not there to watch - he's there toevaluate carefully. Without a clear checklist, feedback becomes "it was cool" or "it needs to be improved", which is useless.

Visual checklist

📋 Observer checklist

1. Opening First 60s
  • ▸ Did you present yourself clearly and objectively?
  • ▸ Did you establish rapport without fuss?
  • ▸ Did you define the purpose of the conversation?

Reveals: initial security and ability to create quick connection

2. Questions Investigation
  • ▸ Did you ask open-ended questions to understand the situation?
  • ▸ Did you deepen the pain with impactful questions?
  • ▸ Did you identify the customer’s desire/future state?

Reveals: capacity for investigation and empathy

3. Listen Attention
  • ▸ Did you listen without interrupting?
  • ▸ Did you use the customer’s words in your response?
  • ▸ Did you confirm understanding before proceeding?

Reveals: active listening and respect for the customer

4. Transition Fluidity
  • ▸ Was the transition from investigation to presentation natural?
  • ▸ Did you connect the customer’s pain with the solution?
  • ▸ Did you ask for permission to present?

Reveals: mastery in conducting the conversation

5. Value Impact
  • ▸ Did you talk about benefits, not features?
  • ▸ Did you connect value with the customer’s specific pain?
  • ▸ Did you use social proof or a concrete example?

Reveals: ability to translate product into solution

6. Closing Conversion
  • ▸ Did you propose a clear next step?
  • ▸ Did you handle the objection well?
  • ▸ Did you close with confidence, without despair?

Reveals: security to lead to commitment

🏋️ Exercise

Print or copy this checklist and use it in the next simulation. For each item, give a score from 1 to 5. Calculate the average. This is the seller's "execution note". Repeat weekly and monitor progress.

💬

4. How to give constructive feedback

The technique that really changes behavior

Forget the "sandwich" technique (praise + criticism + praise). Everyone already knows it and no one takes it seriously. When someone starts with "it was very good, but...", the person already knows that they are criticizing and dismisses the compliment as false.

Feedback that changes behavior follows a more honest and direct format:specific observation + impact + concrete suggestion.

Formula: OIS (Observation + Impact + Suggestion)

OBSERVATION

"When the customer said he thought it was expensive, you immediately offered a discount."

Describe what happened. Fact, no judgement.

IMPACT

"This sent the message that the price was not fair and gave the customer more negotiating power."

Explain the consequence. What does that behavior cause?

SUGGESTION

"Next time, before you talk about price, ask: 'Expensive compared to what?' or 'What would make the investment worth it for you?'"

A practical and specific alternative. Don't say "improve" — say how.

❌ Useless feedback

  • "It was good, but it can get better."
  • "You need to be more confident."
  • "There was no power."
  • "Try to be more natural."

Vague, generic, impossible to apply.

✅ Useful feedback

  • "In minute 3, you skipped the question about financial impact. This left the value of the solution abstract."
  • "When you confirmed the client's pain, he opened up more. Do this more often."
  • "Your transition was abrupt — try using 'based on what you told me...'"

Specific, actionable, fact-based.

💡 Rule of thumb

Focus on behaviors, not personality. "You didn't ask the impact question" is correctable. "You're not good with people" is destructive and untrue. The purpose of feedback is to change what a person DOES, not what they ARE.

TOPIC 5
📹

5. Record and review

The fastest method of progress in sales

If you could adopt just ONE training practice, it would be this:record your simulations and watch together. This method is so effective that elite athletes, professional musicians and airline pilots have been using it for decades. In sales, almost no one does it — and that's exactly why those who do it evolve so quickly.

The reason is neurological: when you watch yourself, you activate different areas of the brain than when you are "in the heat of the moment." You notice pauses, blunders, facial expressions and mistakes that are completely invisible during the action.

How to implement

1

Record the simulation

Use your cell phone. Place it against something stable. Video is ideal, but audio already works very well. No need for professional equipment.

2

Watch together (not alone)

The power is in watching together with those who observed or participated. The conversation during the review generates insights that neither of you would have had alone.

3

Pause at critical points

When you notice a mistake or hit, pause the video. Discuss what happened and what could be different. Then continue.

4

Write down 2-3 improvements

Don't try to fix everything at once. Choose the 2-3 most impactful points and focus on them in the next round.

What people discover by watching themselves

😱 "I talk very quickly when I get nervous"
😱 "I interrupt the customer without realizing it"
😱 "I repeat the same word 15 times"
😱 "I don't look people in the eye when I talk about price"
😱 "I jump straight to the offer without asking"
😱 "I apologize before saying the price"

🏋️ Challenge

Record your next simulation or even a real service (with consent). Watch it alone first and write down what you notice. Then watch with a colleague. I guarantee you will discover at least 3 things that you would never have noticed without the recording.
