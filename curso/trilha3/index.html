Module 3.1

🧬 The 4 factors of the business profile

Understand the 4 factors that determine a professional's commercial potential: aptitude, desire, learning and execution.

What is it:

Natural aptitude is the innate facility for communicating, persuasion and reading people. It includes charisma, empathy, the ability to create rapport and the ability to adapt the speech to the interlocutor.

Why learn:

Identifying natural aptitude allows you to understand each salesperson’s starting point. Those with high aptitude can be accelerated. Those who are discharged need more method and structured training.

Key concepts:

Interpersonal communication, natural persuasion, reading people, rapport, charisma, commercial empathy.

What is it:

Willingness to sell and the genuine desire to be in the commercial area. It includes enjoying the sales game, enduring rejections ("naos"), being hungry for goals and taking pleasure in closing deals.

Why learn:

The will and the fuel. Without it, even the most talented give up. With it, even those with less aptitude find ways to perform.

Key concepts:

Intrinsic motivation, commercial resilience, tolerance to rejection, hunger for goals, pleasure in closing.

What is it:

Ability to learn and the ability to receive feedback, adjust sales pitch and evolve quickly. Includes coachability, technical humility and speed of adaptation.

Why learn:

Salespeople who learn quickly make up for any aptitude gaps. It is the most underestimated and most determining factor in the medium term.

Key concepts:

Coachability, feedback loop, speech adjustment, rapid evolution, technical humility, growth mindset.

What is it:

Consistent execution and the ability to transform potential into routine and results. It's not enough to know how to sell on good days - you need to maintain the standard every day, with discipline and process.

Why learn:

Execution separates those who have potential from those who deliver results. Many talented salespeople fail due to lack of consistency in their routine.

Key concepts:

Commercial discipline, sales routine, consistency, daily process, pipeline management, systematic follow-up.

What is it:

The potential formula combines the 4 factors with specific weights: Aptitude (35%) + Will (25%) + learning (20%) + Execution (20%). Generates a unique score that allows you to compare and classify sellers.

Why learn:

Having an objective formula eliminates guesswork in team evaluation. Allows decisions based on data, not personal preferences or superficial impressions.

Key concepts:

Weighted score, weight of factors, objective assessment, classification by potential, data-based decision.

Module 3.2
3.2 ~50 min

📊 The Aptitude x Will quadrant

Map each salesperson in a quadrant that combines natural aptitude with the desire to sell to define the development strategy.

What is it:

Professional who combines natural sales talent with a genuine desire to sell. And the ideal profile: he learns quickly, motivates himself and delivers results from an early age.

Why learn:

Identifying this profile allows you to accelerate development and provide greater challenges quickly. The most common mistake is not taking advantage of this potential and losing the professional due to lack of challenge.

Key concepts:

Natural salesman, accelerated profile, high potential, fast track, challenge as retention.

What is it:

Professional who has a natural talent for communication and persuasion, but does not want to sell. You may be prejudiced against sales, be in the wrong role, or have had previous negative experiences.

Why learn:

This profile is the most frustrating for managers. The potential is there, but resistance prevents results. It is necessary to understand the root of the rejection before investing in development.

Key concepts:

Commercial resistance, sales bias, role misalignment, wasted talent, blocking investigation.

What is it:

Professional who is not naturally good at sales, but really wants to learn and perform. And dedicated, accepts feedback and tries hard. Needs method and structured training.

Why learn:

This profile is often underestimated. With the right method, you can become an excellent salesperson. The will compensates for the lack of natural aptitude in the medium and long term.

Key concepts:

Built salesperson, development by method, will as a driver, intensive training, learning curve.

What is it:

Professional who has no natural aptitude or desire to be in sales. He may be excellent in other functions, but the commercial area is not the right place for him.

Why learn:

Recognizing this profile early avoids investing time and resources in development that will not yield a return. The best way is to redirect to another area where the professional can shine.

Key concepts:

Commercial fit, redirection, relocation, honesty in diagnosis, cost of keeping it in the wrong place.

Module 3.3
3.3 ~70 min

📝 Evaluation matrix with score

Practical tool with 20 questions to evaluate each factor and generate an objective classification score.

What is it:

Five questions that measure the salesperson's natural aptitude: ease of communication, ability to persuade, reading people, adapting speech and natural charisma. Each question is graded from 1 to 5.

Why learn:

Having standardized questions ensures that the assessment is consistent across different assessors and times. It eliminates the "I think he's good" bias.

Key concepts:

Standardized assessment, scale 1-5, communication, persuasion, reading people, adaptation, charisma.

What is it:

Five questions that measure the desire to sell: pleasure in the commercial game, tolerance for rejection, hunger for a goal, self-motivation and persistence. Each question is graded from 1 to 5.

Why learn:

Will is the factor that most determines the permanence and evolution of a salesperson. Measuring this objectively avoids surprises such as unexpected dropouts.

Key concepts:

Motivation, resilience, goal hunger, self-motivation, persistence, commercial game.

What is it:

Five questions that measure learning capacity: receptivity to feedback, speed of adjustment, active search for knowledge, application of what has been learned and visible evolution. Each question is graded from 1 to 5.

Why learn:

The ability to learn is the best predictor of future evolution. A salesperson who learns quickly today will be a top performer tomorrow, regardless of the starting point.

Key concepts:

Coachability, feedback, speed of adjustment, search for knowledge, practical application, measurable evolution.

What is it:

Five questions that measure execution: routine discipline, completion of activities, pipeline management, consistent follow-up and delivery of results. Each question is graded from 1 to 5.

Why learn:

Execution is what transforms potential into results. Without measuring execution, you could be investing in salespeople who know but don't do it.

Key concepts:

Discipline, commercial routine, pipeline, follow-up, consistency, delivery of results.

What is it:

Score classification system: 21-25 points = high (highlight in the factor), 14-20 points = medium (potential with room to grow), 5-13 points = low (need for development or redirection).

Why learn:

The correct interpretation of scores is as important as the evaluation. An average score is not bad - it depends on the context and the combination with other factors.

Key concepts:

Classification ranges, contextual interpretation, combination of factors, weighted score, holistic view.

🏷️ Groups A, B, C and D

Classify sellers into 4 groups with specific development strategies for each profile.

What is it:

Salespeople with high aptitude and medium or high desire. They are natural talents who already demonstrate ease in sales. The strategy is to develop for maximum performance with increasing challenges.

Why learn:

These professionals are the driving force of the team. If managed well, they quickly become top performers. If poorly managed, they become demotivated and leave.

Key concepts:

Acceleration, challenge as retention, advanced mentoring, ambitious goals, progressive autonomy.

What is it:

Salespeople with medium or low aptitude, but with high desire and learning capacity. They weren't born ready, but they really want to and learn quickly. The strategy is to develop with method.

Why learn:

This group is where the manager generates the most impact. With structured training, they can become excellent salespeople. They are often more loyal and grateful for the investment.

Key concepts:

Development by method, intensive training, scripts and playbooks, close monitoring, gradual evolution.

What is it:

Salespeople who may have an aptitude for sales but reject the activity. Resistance can come from prejudices, bad experiences or misalignment of values. The strategy is understanding the block.

Why learn:

Before giving up on this profile, it is necessary to investigate the cause of the resistance. Sometimes the blockage is removable (e.g., limiting belief about sales). Other times, it is definitive.

Key concepts:

Blocking investigation, limiting beliefs, reframing sales, frank conversation, decision deadline.

What is it:

Salespeople with low aptitude and low desire to sell. It is not a value judgment - the person can be excellent in other functions. The strategy is to redirect with respect and clarity.

Why learn:

Keeping someone without a commercial fit on the sales team harms the person and the team. Redirecting early is an act of respect, not exclusion.

Key concepts:

Redirection, relocation, humanized conversation, opportunity cost, professional fit.

Module 3.5
3.5 ~65 min

🔬 Diagnosis in practice

How to apply the assessment, give feedback and avoid common mistakes when classifying the sales team.

What is it:

Practical guide on how to apply the evaluation: best time (after 30-60 days of work), environment (reserved and calm), format (individual, confidential) and who should evaluate (direct manager + self-evaluation).

Why learn:

The best assessment tool fails if applied in the wrong context. The moment and environment directly impact the quality of responses.

Key concepts:

Assessment timing, safe environment, confidentiality, cross-self assessment, first diagnosis.

What is it:

Techniques for sharing the evaluation results with the seller in a transparent but constructive way. Includes feedback framework: strengths first, areas for development, joint action plan.

Why learn:

A bad return can destroy the motivation of a good salesperson. A good feedback can transform an average seller into an excellent one.

Key concepts:

Constructive feedback, structured feedback, joint action plan, respectful transparency, focus on development.

What is it:

The most common mistakes: halo effect (one quality contaminates everything), recency bias (only remembers the last month), favoritism, confusing sympathy with aptitude, and projecting your own qualities without being evaluated.

Why learn:

Knowing biases is the first step to avoiding them. A biased diagnosis is worse than no diagnosis, because it generates false confidence in wrong decisions.

Key concepts:

Halo effect, recency bias, favoritism, projection, anchoring, confirmation, similarity bias.

What is it:

Signs that the classification needs to be revised: results consistently above or below expectations for the group, changes in behavior, personal events that changed motivation.

Why learn:

No diagnosis is permanent. People change, contexts change. Knowing when to reevaluate prevents you from treating someone based on a photo that no longer reflects reality.

Key concepts:

Reassessment, signs of change, dynamic diagnosis, flexibility, profile update.

What is it:

Complete case study: a team of 6 salespeople evaluated with the matrix, classified into groups and with individual action plans. Shows the process from start to finish with real results.

Why learn:

Seeing the process applied in a real case connects theory to practice. You understand the nuances that only appear in the application and are prepared to make your own diagnosis.

Key concepts:

Practical case, real application, nuances of diagnosis, individual action plan, results after intervention.

🔄 Reevaluation and evolution

When and how to reassess, measure progress, change groups and keep the diagnosis alive and updated.

What is it:

Defines the ideal frequency of reassessment (quarterly as standard) and the triggers that require immediate reassessment: sudden change in performance, relevant personal event, change of role or territory.

Why learn:

Diagnosis without reevaluation becomes a label. It is necessary to keep the process alive so that it continues to be a development tool, not a rigidity tool.

Key concepts:

Quarterly frequency, reassessment triggers, dynamic diagnosis, anti-label, live process.

What is it:

Specific indicators to measure evolution in each factor: aptitude (conversion rate), will (proactivity and volume), learning (speed of adjustment after feedback) and execution (adherence to the process).

Why learn:

Without clear indicators, progress becomes opinion. With indicators, you can show the seller exactly where he is improving and where he needs to improve.

Key concepts:

KPIs by factor, evolution measurement, baseline, measurable progress, historical scores.

What is it:

Objective criteria for promoting or demoting someone from a group: minimum 2 consecutive assessments with a score compatible with the new group, consistent behavioral evidence and aligned results.

Why learn:

Changing groups without discretion generates instability and distrust. Having clear rules of credibility for the process shows that evolution is possible and recognized.

Key concepts:

Transition criteria, two consecutive assessments, behavioral evidence, mobility between groups, recognition of evolution.

What is it:

The summary of the entire trail: "Talent helps you get in quickly, desire and learning determine who becomes good." Fitness accelerates the start, but does not guarantee staying at the top. Will and learning are the real long-term differentiators.

Why learn:

This phrase encapsulates the philosophy of commercial diagnosis and should guide all team development decisions. And the manager's compass.

Key concepts:

Talent vs will, short vs long term, performance sustainability, diagnosis philosophy, manager’s compass.
