🏗️ The role of the commercial manager

Understand why the manager is the architect of the team's results, not just a goal collector.

What is it:

The effective commercial manager doesn't just demand numbers — he designs the environment, processes and development that make results inevitable. Demanding a goal is a consequence, not a cause.

Why learn:

Managers who only charge create stressed and rotating teams. Managers who build create teams that achieve goals in a sustainable way.

Key concepts:

Results architecture, proactive vs reactive management, high performance environment, leadership by construction.

What is it:

The manager has three central functions: diagnosing the profile of each salesperson, positioning him in the right role and training him with the method appropriate to his level.

Why learn:

Without diagnosis, you treat everyone the same. Without positioning, you place the wrong people in the wrong role. Without training, you expect results without investing in the process.

Key concepts:

Profile diagnosis, strategic positioning, personalized training, individualized management.

What is it:

Not every salesperson works well with every type of customer. The effective manager crosses the salesperson's profile with the customer's level of awareness to maximize conversion.

Why learn:

Placing the right salesperson with the right customer can double the conversion rate without any additional training.

Key concepts:

Seller-customer match, awareness level, strategic allocation, portfolio optimization.

What is it:

The manager who trains invests in development, creates processes and multiplies capacity. The manager who demands focuses on pressure, collection and volume. Both generate results, but at very different costs.

Why learn:

The charge works in the short term but destroys it in the long term. Training takes longer but creates autonomous and sustainable teams.

Key concepts:

Training leadership vs collector, sustainability, turnover, team development, autonomy.

What is it:

An effective manager has a clear routine: pipeline analysis, individual monitoring, structured feedback, simulations and strategic planning. It's not improvisation.

Why learn:

Without a routine, the manager lives putting out fires. Routinely, he anticipates problems and creates conditions for the team to perform.

Key concepts:

Management routine, cadence, pipeline review, 1:1, weekly planning, proactive management.

⭐ Track for the talented salesperson

How to develop those who already have a natural talent for sales and take them to the next level.

What is it:

The talented salesperson has a natural ability to connect and persuade, but often operates on instinct. He needs to learn a method to be consistent.

Why learn:

Unmethodical and inconsistent talent. The talented person who learns the process becomes unbeatable.

Key concepts:

Talent vs method, consistency, gaps in the intuitive salesperson, structuring instinct.

What is it:

Add frameworks and structures to the talented salesperson’s natural repertoire. It’s not about plastering it — it’s about providing tools that amplify what already works.

Why learn:

Talent with method can replicate their best days every day. Without a method, he oscillates between genius and mediocrity.

Key concepts:

Technical refinement, sales frameworks, structuring without rigidity, talent amplification.

What is it:

Talented people need freedom to operate, but with clear indicators of responsibility. Autonomy is not the absence of monitoring — it is trust with accountability.

Why learn:

Micromanaging the talented demotivates them. Leaving unattended creates a comfort zone. Balance is the key.

Key concepts:

Responsible autonomy, accountability, result indicators, management by exception.

What is it:

The talented person who is not challenged gets bored and seeks new horizons. Progressive goals, special projects and increasing responsibilities maintain engagement.

Why learn:

Losing a talented salesperson is one of the biggest invisible costs of a business operation. Challenge is the retention tool.

Key concepts:

Stretch goals, engagement, talent retention, progressive challenges, career plan.

What is it:

The transition from good to excellent requires advanced mentoring, exposure to complex scenarios and development of leadership and influencing skills.

Why learn:

High-performance salespeople don't just sell more — they raise the standard of the entire team and become a reference.

Key concepts:

High performance, mentoring, exposure to complexity, informal leadership, team reference.

📏Trail for the trainable salesperson

The most important group on the team: how discipline and process transform potential into results.

What is it:

Trainables are typically 60-70% of the team. He doesn't have exceptional natural talent, but he has discipline and a desire to learn. Investing in this group is the biggest management ROI.

Why learn:

Many managers focus on talent and ignore trainable talent. That's a mistake — the team's growth comes from this group.

Key concepts:

Development ROI, trainable majority, discipline vs talent, investment in people.

What is it:

The script starts as a crutch — the salesperson reads it and follows it. With repetition, internalize it. With mastery, adapt. The objective is not to robotize, but to create a solid base that becomes natural.

Why learn:

Without a script, the trainable person improvises poorly. With scripting, it has a safe starting point that evolves into real skill.

Key concepts:

Sales script, internalization, deliberate repetition, evolution of the script to fluency.

What is it:

The trainable needs more frequent monitoring: listening to calls, reviewing proposals, daily feedback at the beginning and weekly as it evolves.

Why learn:

Late feedback does not correct habits. Frequent feedback accelerates learning exponentially.

Key concepts:

Feedback cadence, active listening, pipeline review, close coaching, quick correction.

What is it:

Weekly simulations of real sales scenarios. The coachable person practices objections, negotiations and closings in a safe environment before facing the real client.

Why learn:

Role play is the equivalent of athlete training. Nobody competes without training. The salesperson who simulates makes fewer mistakes in real time.

Key concepts:

Role play, sales simulation, objection scenarios, deliberate practice, safe environment.

What is it:

Trainees need a clear and detailed process: how many calls, when to follow-up, how to register in the CRM, when to escalate. Ambiguity is the enemy of this profile.

Why learn:

Clear process generates predictability. Trainable with a defined process delivers consistent and measurable results.

Key concepts:

Sales process, playbook, commercial cadence, predictability, activity metrics.

🔄 Tough Profile Track

How to reposition those who resist the traditional sales model into roles that generate value.

What is it:

Before labeling someone as resistant, I need to investigate: does the person reject sales per se or reject the current model? Often the resistance is to the method, not the function.

Why learn:

Misdiagnosing leads to unnecessary dismissals. Diagnosing it right can turn a resister into a valuable contributor.

Key concepts:

Diagnosis of resistance, root cause, rejection of the model vs rejection of sales, empathetic investigation.

What is it:

Pre-sales is a function where the professional qualifies leads, schedules meetings and prepares opportunities without the pressure of closing. Ideal for those who are good at relationships but not at negotiation.

Why learn:

Many resisters are excellent in qualification. Positioning them in pre-sales takes advantage of their strengths without exposing their weaknesses.

Key concepts:

Pre-sales, SDR, lead qualification, separation of functions, commercial specialization.

What is it:

Produce educational content, maintain relationships with customers and nurture leads through value — not pressure. A way to sell indirectly.

Why learn:

The resistant person who hates calling to sell can be excellent at educating the market. Content and relationships generate sales without a direct approach.

Key concepts:

Consultative selling, inbound, content marketing, nurturing, long-term relationships.

What is it:

Social selling is using social networks to build authority, generate trust and attract customers organically. It's not spam — it's strategic presence.

Why learn:

Resistant profiles who communicate well in writing can be excellent social sellers — generating demand without a cold approach.

Key concepts:

Social selling, LinkedIn, building authority, social inbound, strategic digital presence.

What is it:

Technical and strategic support function for the sales team. The internal consultant helps prepare proposals, analyzes scenarios and guides approaches without being on the front line.

Why learn:

Not all business knowledge needs to be on the front lines. The internal consultant multiplies the team's capacity.

Key concepts:

Internal consultancy, sales support, sales enablement, technical specialist, multiplier.

🚪 When to redirect someone

Recognize when it won't work and act with clarity, respect and the right timing.

What is it:

Clear indicators that the investment is not generating a return: falling activity metrics, resistance to feedback, lack of evolution after specific interventions.

Why learn:

Keeping someone in the wrong role harms the person and the team. Recognizing the signs early prevents further damage.

Key concepts:

Red flags, performance indicators, plateau, resistance to change, objective assessment.

What is it:

Set clear development deadlines with measurable milestones. Typically 60-90 days for new sellers, with biweekly checkpoints.

Why learn:

Giving too much time wears out. Giving too little time is unfair. Deadlines with milestones generate transparency and fairness in the assessment.

Key concepts:

Ramp-up, adaptation period, development milestones, checkpoints, PIP (performance improvement plan).

What is it:

The art of communicating redirection with honesty, empathy and focus on the future. It's not about failure — it's about finding the right place for each person.

Why learn:

Poorly conducted conversations create trauma, labor lawsuits and a negative reputation. Well-conducted conversations maintain dignity and open doors.

Key concepts:

Difficult feedback, non-violent communication, redirection, career talk, assertive empathy.

What is it:

Map possible destinations: customer success, operations, marketing, support, partners. Not every sales exit is a company exit.

Why learn:

The manager who knows how to redirect keeps talents in the company even when sales is not the right place. This saves hiring costs and preserves knowledge.

Key concepts:

Internal mobility, repositioning, skills map, career transition, talent retention.

📊 The seller x awareness level matrix

The strategic crossover that defines who does what in your sales team.

What is it:

The matrix crosses the 3 salesperson profiles (talented, trainable, resilient) with the 5 levels of customer awareness to define the best allocation of resources.

Why learn:

The matrix eliminates guesswork in the distribution of leads and customers. Each profile has a place where it generates more results.

Key concepts:

Allocation matrix, awareness levels, salesperson profile, resource optimization, commercial strategy.

What is it:

The talented are more effective with low and medium awareness clients, where their ability to create connection and urgency makes the most difference. With very conscious customers, it focuses on differentiators.

Why learn:

Allocating talent to the wrong level wastes potential. Understanding where it shines most allows you to maximize conversion.

Key concepts:

Talent allocation, customer awareness, approach by level, maximizing conversion.

What is it:

Trainable works best with medium to high awareness clients, where well-defined scripts and processes are sufficient. Each level of consciousness requires a different script.

Why learn:

Giving the right script for the right level of the client is what makes the coachable person perform above average without needing exceptional talent.

Key concepts:

Scripts by level, adapted scripts, segmented process, systematic approach.

What is it:

The resistant contributes more in support functions: pre-sales for unaware customers, content to nurture leads, consultancy for very aware customers who need technical depth.

Why learn:

Finding the right resistor fit saves layoffs and preserves valuable knowledge on the team.

Key concepts:

Functional fit, commercial support, complementary functions, indirect contribution.

What is it:

A practical table that crosses profile x level and defines: main action, tool, monitoring metrics and review deadline for each combination.

Why learn:

The table is the most practical instrument on this trail. Allows quick and consistent decisions on team allocation.

Key concepts:

Management template, action table, decision framework, practical allocation, visual management.

🔁 Commercial management routine

The weekly cadence, meetings, follow-ups and practices that sustain long-term results.

What is it:

A fixed weekly structure: Monday for planning, Tuesday to Thursday for monitoring, Friday for review and adjustments. Each day has defined activities.

Why learn:

The weekly cadence eliminates chaos and creates predictability in management. The manager knows what to do at each moment.

Key concepts:

Management cadence, weekly planning, operational rhythm, fixed routine, predictability.

What is it:

Effective business meetings have a defined format: prior agenda, maximum duration, focus on actions and decisions. There is no space for reports — it is space for resolution.

Why learn:

Poorly conducted meetings waste team hours. Well-run meetings accelerate results and align the team.

Key concepts:

Agenda, timeboxing, actionable decisions, pipeline review, forecast meeting.

What is it:

Weekly or biweekly individual meetings with each salesperson. Agenda: results of the week, difficulties, next steps, personal development.

Why learn:

The 1:1 is where the real management happens. And where the manager diagnoses, guides and develops each person individually.

Key concepts:

One-on-one, individual coaching, development plan, active listening, personalized support.

What is it:

Effective and specific feedback, based on evidence, focused on behavior (not on person) and always with suggestions for action. SBI Model: Situation, Behavior, Impact.

Why learn:

Generic feedback doesn't change anything. Specific, actionable feedback transforms behavior and accelerates development.

Key concepts:

SBI model, specific feedback, evidence, corrective action, positive and constructive feedback.

What is it:

Regular role plays and simulations as part of the team's weekly routine. Practice of objections, closings, negotiations and critical scenarios.

Why learn:

Teams that train together evolve together. Simulation creates sales muscle that works automatically in real time.

Key concepts:

Team role play, collective training, critical scenarios, deliberate practice, commercial muscle.
