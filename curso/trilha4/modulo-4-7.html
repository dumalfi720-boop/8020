Commercial management routine

Commercial management does not happen in epic moments. It happens in routine — in small actions repeated every week that create predictability, correct deviations quickly, and build a team that continually improves.

5 Topics ~45 min Management Routine
Topic 1
📅

1. Weekly cadence

What the commercial manager should do every week. Without cadence, management will turn into putting out fires. With cadence, you anticipate problems, correct them quickly and create rhythm in the team.

Manager's weekly agenda template

Monday
Pipeline review. Look at all ongoing businesses: which ones have advanced? Which ones have stagnated? Which ones died? Prioritize critical follow-ups for the week.
Tuesday
Individual monitoring (1:1). Two to three 15-minute conversations with salespeople. Focus: diagnose, guide, unlock. Do not charge — direct.
Wednesday
Individual monitoring (continued). Complete the remaining 1:1. Review call recordings or team calls. Note patterns.
Thursday
Route monitoring and correction. Check whether the guided adjustments are being applied. Intervene in critical businesses if necessary.
Friday
Team simulation (30 min). A scenario from the real week. Everyone practices. End the week with energy and collective learning.
Friday (extra)
Review of metrics for the week: leads generated, proposals sent, closings, losses. Prepare the agenda for the following Monday.

Why cadence matters more than managerial talent

  • ▸Predictability:the team knows what to expect and prepares. No one is caught by surprise.
  • ▸Correction speed:Problems are identified in days, not months. A salesperson stuck on Tuesday already has guidance on Wednesday.
  • ▸Rhythm:Management with rhythm is contagious to the team. When the manager has discipline, the team absorbs discipline.
  • ▸Accumulated data:every week you collect information. In 4 weeks, you have a complete team map.
Topic 2
🤝

2. Meetings that work

Most business meetings are a waste of time. Too long, without an agenda, without concrete action at the end. Here you learn the format that works: short, objective and action-oriented.

Golden rules: 30 minutes, fixed agenda

1
Maximum 30 minutes
If it doesn't fit in 30 minutes, the agenda is wrong. Long meetings dilute attention and no longer generate results. Set a visible timer.
2
Fixed agenda: Results → Blocks → Next steps
Always in that order. First: what happened (data). Second: what is stopping (problems). Third: what each one will do until next time (commitment).
3
Everyone talks, no one speaks
Each seller has 3-5 minutes for their update. No monologues from the manager. If someone needs more time, schedule a separate 1:1.

Agenda template — Weekly team meeting

WEEKLY BUSINESS MEETING — [DATE]
Duration: 30 min | Participants: commercial team
BLOCK 1 — RESULTS (10 min)
- New leads this week: ___
- Proposals sent: ___
- Closings: ___
- Losses: ___
- Total pipeline: R$ ___
BLOCK 2 — BLOCKAGES (10 min)
- What is stopping conversions?
- Any recurring objection patterns?
- Is something missing: material, access, tool?
BLOCK 3 — NEXT STEPS (10 min)
- [Name]: appointment until Friday ___
- [Name]: appointment until Friday ___
- Manager: support action ___

What NOT to do in business meetings

  • ✗Collection meeting without direction:"Why didn't you reach your goal?" without offering any path. This generates fear, not improvement.
  • ✗Manager's monologue:speak 25 of the 30 minutes. The meeting belongs to the team, not the manager.
  • ✗No defined agenda:"let's see what happens" guarantees that nothing will happen. Always send an agenda first.
  • ✗Exhibit individually in public:Individual problems are resolved 1:1, not in front of everyone.
Topic 3
👤

3. Individual monitoring

The weekly 1:1 is the commercial manager’s most powerful tool. In 15-20 minutes, you diagnose, guide and unlock. It's not about charging — it's the moment where the seller grows the most.

Structure of the weekly 1:1 (15-20 min)

Minutes 1-5: What did you sell this week?

Let the seller tell you. Which businesses advanced? Which ones closed? What went right? Start with the positive — this opens the conversation.

Minutes 5-10: What did you miss and why?

Here is the gold. What businesses were lost? At what stage? What was the objection? Can the seller identify where they went wrong? If he can't, help him see.

Minutes 10-15: Diagnosis and guidance

Based on what you heard, make a diagnosis: is the problem technical (don't know how to do it), behavioral (don't want to do it) or structural (can't do it)? Give specific and actionable guidance.

Minutes 15-20: Appointment of the week

What will the seller do differently next week? Just one thing. Specifies. Measurable. "I will use the 4 diagnostic questions in all calls on Wednesday and Thursday."

1:1 WELL DONE

  • ✓ Seller leaves motivated and with direction
  • ✓ Knows exactly what to improve
  • ✓ Feels that the manager is on his side
  • ✓ Has a clear commitment
  • ✓ Wait for the next conversation

1:1 BADLY done

  • ✗ Salesperson leaves unmotivated and afraid
  • ✗ Don't know what to do differently
  • ✗ Feels that the manager is a collector
  • ✗ No defined concrete action
  • ✗ Try to avoid the next conversation

The fundamental difference: diagnosis vs billing

Cobranca says: "You didn't reach the goal." Diagnosis asks: "What happened to the businesses that didn't close?" The charge generates defense. Diagnosis generates learning.

The manager who charges can be replaced by a dashboard. The manager who diagnoses is irreplaceable — he is the coach who improves the player week by week.

Topic 4
💬

4. Feedback that changes behavior

Generic feedback doesn't change anything. "You need to improve" is not feedback — it's noise. What changes behavior is a precise model: specific observation, clear impact and actionable suggestion.

Feedback model: O.I.S.

O
Specific observation

Describe exactly what you saw or heard. No judgment, no generalization. "On the call with customer X on Wednesday, you skipped the diagnostic questions and went straight to pricing."

I
Impact

Explain what that caused. Connect the behavior to the outcome. "This made the customer stop because he still didn't understand the value. He compared directly with the competitor and asked for a discount."

S
Suggestion

Offer a concrete alternative. "Next, try using the 4 diagnostic questions before mentioning price. This builds value before negotiation."

Complete OIS feedback example

"João, I noticed that on the call with Empresa ABC on Wednesday you skipped the diagnosis and went straight to presenting the plan and the price. This made the client stop — he asked 'but why this price?' because I still didn't understand the problem we solve. In the next call, try to use the 4 questions before: what is the current scenario, what have you tried, what is the impact of this, and what would change if it were resolved. This builds value before talking about money. Do you want to practice this now in 5 minutes?"

Result: The seller knows exactly what they did, understands why it didn't work, and has a clear path to improve.

❌
Bad feedback

"You need to sell more."

😐
Average feedback

"You're skipping steps in the call."

✅
OIS Feedback

"In call X, you skipped Y, caused Z. Try W next time."

Topic 5
🎭

5. Simulation as a team routine

Simulation is not "group dynamics" — it's training. Athletes train every day. Salespeople should train every week. 30 minutes of well-conducted simulation are worth more than 8 hours of theory.

Weekly simulation format (30 min)

1
Minutes 1-5: Introduce the scenario
Base it on a real situation from the week. "The client owns a gym, earns R$30k/month, wants to grow but thinks he doesn't have the money. He already did a consultancy that didn't produce results."
2
Minutes 5-15: Live simulation
One salesperson plays the role of the salesperson, another plays the role of the customer. The rest watch. No pauses — it's like a real call. Those who observe note down strengths and areas for improvement.
3
Minutes 15-25: Collective feedback
First the seller evaluates himself. Then the team contributes (using the OIS model). The manager closes with the most important observations.
4
Minutes 25-30: Key Learning
What is the main learning? What should everyone apply this week? Close with a collective commitment. If possible, register in a shared document.

Rotate papers

Each week, different people take on the roles. This is crucial because:

  • ▸ Playing the role of a customer teaches empathy
  • ▸ Observing teaches critical analysis
  • ▸ Selling under social pressure trains emotional control
  • ▸ Everyone learns from everyone

Record when possible

Recording enhances learning because:

  • ▸ The seller sees himself as an outsider (self-knowledge)
  • ▸ You can review at another time
  • ▸ Creates a bank of examples for new sellers
  • ▸ Allows you to compare progress over the months

Suggested scenarios per week (4 week cycle)

Without 1: Customer who says "it's expensive". Train value construction and price anchoring.
Without 2: Client who says “I need to think”. Train emergency techniques and next step commitment.
Without 3: Cold customer — never heard of you. Train openness, rapport and diagnosis.
Without 4: Customer who has already purchased from a competitor and was frustrated. Training differentiation and rebuilding trust.
