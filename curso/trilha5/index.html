🙈 Strategy for the Unconscious Public

Reach those who don't yet know they have a problem. Education as a gateway.

What is it:

The unconscious public doesn't know they have a problem. You think your situation is normal. He doesn't look for solutions because he doesn't realize he needs one. It is the furthest level from purchase.

Why learn:

The majority of the market is at this level. If you only talk to those who already want to buy, you miss 80% of the opportunities. Knowing how to awaken awareness is the biggest strategic difference.

Key concepts:

Level of awareness, cold public, market education, awakening pain, normalization of the problem.

What is it:

Educate the market and create content that makes people think “wow, this happens to me”. It's not about selling — it's about making the person recognize the problem before they know there is a solution.

Why learn:

Selling to those who don't know they need it is impossible. Educating is the only way to move the unconscious to the next level of consciousness.

Key concepts:

Educational marketing, identification content, mirror of the problem, strategic empathy.

What is it:

For the unconscious, stories that mirror his situation, shocking data that reveal the problem and before/after comparisons that show what he doesn't see work.

Why learn:

Technical content does not work for unconscious people. They need emotional and relational content. Knowing the right format saves time and increases conversion.

Key concepts:

Storytelling, impact data, visual comparison, relational content, identification trigger.

What is it:

The unconscious public is not actively searching. He is on social networks, in groups, consuming entertainment content. You need to go to him, not wait for him to come.

Why learn:

Investing in Google Ads is unconscious and throwing money away. Each level of consciousness has ideal channels. Getting the channel wrong and wasting budget.

Key concepts:

Paid vs organic media, interruption vs intent, social media, cold traffic, reach.

What is it:

Real examples of posts, advertisements, videos and messages that work for the unconscious public. Templates ready to adapt to your niche and product.

Why learn:

Theory without example and too abstract. Seeing real copy that works speeds up the learning curve and gives you models to replicate immediately.

Key concepts:

Copywriting for top of the funnel, identification headlines, educational CTAs, warm-up sequences.

😣 Strategy for Awareness of the Problem

Deepen the pain of those who already know they have a problem but don't know the solution.

What is it:

The person feels the pain but doesn't know what to do. She searches for information, reads articles, watches videos. You're in "discovery" mode — open to learning but haven't yet decided to act.

Why learn:

This is the level where most qualified leads are. Knowing how to communicate here is essential to not miss real conversation opportunities.

Key concepts:

Awareness of the problem, active search, recognized pain, latent interest, natural qualification.

What is it:

Show the real cost of not solving the problem. How much money do you lose per month? How much time wasted? What opportunities slip away? Quantifying inaction creates urgency.

Why learn:

People tolerate chronic pain. When you show the accumulated cost, tolerance turns into a need to act.

Key concepts:

Cost of inaction, quantified pain, projection of loss, trigger of internal urgency.

What is it:

Design future scenarios if the person does not solve the problem. Where will she be in 6 months, 1 year, 5 years maintaining the same behavior.

Why learn:

The negative future is a powerful motivator. When a person visualizes the consequences, the urgency to act increases significantly.

Key concepts:

Negative projection, future scenario, visible consequence, motivation due to loss aversion.

What is it:

Internal urgency is different from external pressure. It's not "buy it now" — it's making the person feel like they need to act on their own persuasion, not manipulation.

Why learn:

Artificial urgency breeds distrust. Internal urgency generates sustainable action and more qualified customers who buy out of conviction.

Key concepts:

Internal vs external urgency, self-conviction, intrinsic motivation, conscious decision.

What is it:

Real examples of content for those aware of the problem: posts that deepen the pain, videos that show consequences, emails that quantify losses.

Why learn:

Ready-made approach models reduce creation time and increase efficiency. See what works and the shortcut to results.

Key concepts:

Deep-dive copy, mid-funnel content, nurturing sequence, awareness automation.

🔎 Solution Aware Strategy

Position yourself as the best option for those who already know that there are paths.

What is it:

The person knows that there are solutions to their problem, but they don't know you yet. He is researching options, comparing approaches, evaluating possible paths.

Why learn:

This is the time to appear as a reference. If you don't position yourself here, the competitor captures this lead.

Key concepts:

Solution awareness, active research, comparison of options, strategic positioning.

What is it:

Communicate what makes your approach unique. It's not about being "better" generically — it's about showing the method, the perspective, the specific result that only you deliver.

Why learn:

In a saturated market, those who do not differentiate themselves become commodities. Clear differentiators attract the right customers and drive away the wrong ones.

Key concepts:

Unique value proposition, differentiation, positioning, unique mechanism, proprietary approach.

What is it:

Compare solution categories without demonizing competitors. "There are 3 paths: A, B and C. We use C for these reasons." Elegance generates more confidence than aggression.

Why learn:

Attacking a competitor generates distrust and looks like desperation. Positioning yourself with class shows security and professionalism.

Key concepts:

Category comparison, positioning by contrast, competitive elegance, education about options.

What is it:

Content that demonstrates in-depth mastery of the subject: detailed analyses, case studies, proprietary frameworks, reasoned opinions that only an expert would give.

Why learn:

Authority is not declared, it is perceived. The right content positions you as a reference without needing to proclaim yourself an expert.

Key concepts:

Authority marketing, thought leadership, in-depth content, proof of competence.

What is it:

Content templates for those aware of the solution: comparisons, market analyses, case studies, positioning webinars.

Why learn:

Concrete examples eliminate the doubt of “how to do it” and allow immediate application in your business.

Key concepts:

Positioning copy, comparative content, authority sequence, consideration funnel.

🛒 Strategy for Product Awareness

Convince those who already know your offer and are comparing it to alternatives.

What is it:

The person knows your product or service. You've seen your offer, you know the price, you understand what you do. But he is still evaluating — comparing with other options, seeking confidence to decide.

Why learn:

This is the most critical moment in the funnel. A mistake here loses the lead to the competitor. Communication needs to eliminate objections and generate trust.

Key concepts:

Product awareness, comparison phase, elimination of objections, decision trigger.

What is it:

Testimonials, case studies and results from real clients. Numbers, prints, videos of satisfied customers. Proof that other people have already had success with you.

Why learn:

Social proof is the most powerful trigger at this level. People trust other customers more than they trust you. A good testimony is worth a thousand arguments.

Key concepts:

Social proof, testimonials, case studies, tangible results, third-party endorsement.

What is it:

Satisfaction guarantees, trial periods, free demonstrations, samples, trials. Anything that reduces the fear of “what if it doesn’t work for me?”

Why learn:

The fear of making mistakes is the main purchasing barrier. Those who reduce perceived risk convert more, even at higher prices.

Key concepts:

Risk reversal, guarantee, trial, demonstration, friction reduction, risk inversion.

What is it:

Credentials, experience, awards, media appearances, partnerships, number of clients served. Anything that communicates “you are in good hands.”

Why learn:

Trust is the last filter before purchasing. Without trust, even the best offer fails. Built authority eliminates final hesitation.

Key concepts:

Perceived authority, trust, credibility, endorsement, reputation, track record.

What is it:

Sales page templates, conversational email sequences, follow-up scripts and social proof formats that convert product awareness.

Why learn:

This level requires surgical precision in communication. Ready-made examples eliminate trial and error and speed up conversions.

Key concepts:

Conversion copy, sales page, follow-up, closing sequence, decision triggers.

🚀 Strategy for Very Conscious

Lead those who are ready to buy to closing.

What is it:

The person has already decided that they want to buy from you. He knows the product, he trusts you, he knows the price. All that’s missing is a final trigger — a condition, a bonus, a deadline, a special offer.

Why learn:

Losing a very conscious person is the worst waste of the funnel. The sale is there — it just needs to be harvested. Wrong communication here can alienate those who were ready.

Key concepts:

Very conscious, ready to purchase, final trigger, facilitating decision, closing.

What is it:

For the very conscious, clarity and all. Price, condition, what is included, deadline, payment method. No fluff, no long sales letters. Straight to the point.

Why learn:

Overcomplicating the offer for those who already want to buy creates friction. Simplicity and clarity are the biggest accelerators at this level.

Key concepts:

Direct offer, clarity of proposal, simplicity, special conditions, ease of purchase.

What is it:

Real and fact-based urgency: limited places, bonus for time, price that will increase, class that closes. It's not manipulation — it's reality that motivates action.

Why learn:

Without urgency, the very conscious procrastinates. "I'll buy later" is the silent sales killer. Real urgency turns intention into action.

Key concepts:

Real scarcity, deadline, temporal bonus, legitimate FOMO, decision accelerator.

What is it:

The CTA (Call to Action) is very conscious and direct: "Buy now", "Secure your spot", "Start today". No hesitation, no “learn more”. The next step is clear and unique.

Why learn:

A weak CTA loses guaranteed sales. The very conscious wants you to make the purchase easier, not create more steps.

Key concepts:

Direct CTA, purchase button, facilitation, checkout, click reduction, clear command.

What is it:

Examples of closing emails, WhatsApp messages, optimized checkout pages and sales scripts for the very conscious.

Why learn:

The closing and where the money comes in. Having ready-made, high-conversion models is essential for any commercial operation.

Key concepts:

Closing copy, last chance email, offer you can’t refuse, final conversion sequence.

📋 Content Plan for All Levels

Create a complete content plan that meets all levels of awareness.

What is it:

60% of the content must educate, entertain and generate value. 40% can be a direct offer. This proportion keeps the audience engaged without being invasive.

Why learn:

Those who only sell keep away. Those who only educate never convert. The 60/40 balance is the sweet spot proven by content marketers.

Key concepts:

Content/offer ratio, content marketing, value before sale, editorial balance.

What is it:

Each channel has its ideal format: Instagram for visuals, YouTube for in-depth information, email for conversation, WhatsApp for relationships. Adapting content to the channel multiplies results.

Why learn:

Publish the same content on all channels and waste potential. Each platform has its own language and its audience at different levels of awareness.

Key concepts:

Multichannel distribution, native format, content adaptation, platform strategy.

What is it:

A weekly calendar that distributes content for each level of consciousness. Second: top of the funnel. Tuesday: middle. Fourth: authority. Fifth: social proof. Friday: offer.

Why learn:

Without a calendar, content production is chaotic. With a calendar, it becomes a system. And systems scale.

Key concepts:

Editorial calendar, weekly planning, distribution by level, publication consistency.

What is it:

Each level of consciousness responds better to different formats. Unconscious: short video. Aware of the problem: articles. Very aware: direct email. The ideal frequency varies by channel.

Why learn:

Wrong format = wrong audience. Wrong frequency = irrelevance or saturation. Getting both right maximizes content ROI.

Key concepts:

Content format, publication frequency, multimedia, format adaptation, content ROI.

What is it:

Top-of-the-funnel emails can be automated. Closing a complex sale requires human contact. Knowing what to automate and what to customize is decisive for scaling with quality.

Why learn:

Automating everything loses the human connection. Doing everything manually doesn't scale. Balance is the secret to profitable operations.

Key concepts:

Marketing automation, personalization, scale vs quality, human touchpoints.

What is it:

A complete template with all the necessary fields: awareness level, channel, format, frequency, theme, CTA, follow-up metrics. Ready to copy and adapt.

Why learn:

Having a template avoids starting from scratch. It's the shortcut between learning the theory and immediately applying it in practice.

Key concepts:

Content template, editorial plan, publishing framework, content system, monthly planning.

🤝 Integrating Team and Strategy

Set up an integrated operation: marketing, sales and management aligned by level of awareness.

What is it:

Each level of awareness requires a different salesperson profile. Unconscious people need educators. Product conscious people need closers. Very aware they need facilitators.

Why learn:

Putting the wrong salesperson at the wrong level will waste talent and lose sales. Profile alignment with level multiplies results.

Key concepts:

Seller profile, hunter vs farmer, closer vs consultant, team alignment.

What is it:

Marketing leads at the initial levels (unconscious and problem-aware) with content and education. At the final levels, marketing supports sales with collateral and social proof.

Why learn:

Defining where marketing leads and where marketing supports eliminates conflicts between departments and optimizes resources.

Key concepts:

Top-of-funnel marketing, sales enablement, support materials, nurturing, lead qualification.

What is it:

Salespeople enter forcefully from the awareness of the solution. At initial levels, they act as consultants. In product awareness, as negotiators. Not very conscious, as facilitators.

Why learn:

Salesperson who tries to close an unconscious person burns the lead. A seller who consults a very conscientious person loses the sale. Each level requires a different posture.

Key concepts:

Consultative selling, negotiation, closing, facilitation, posture by level, adaptive approach.

What is it:

Signs that it’s time to scale: stable conversion rates, validated process, trained team, clear metrics. Climb without these signals and amplify problems.

Why learn:

Scaling too early breaks the operation. Climbing too late misses an opportunity. Knowing the right moment is just as important as knowing how to climb.

Key concepts:

Scale, process validation, readiness metrics, growth, growth timing.

What is it:

The 8020 integrated model: marketing generates awareness, sales converts, management optimizes. All aligned by the same framework of levels of consciousness. A single language for the entire operation.

Why learn:

Companies where marketing and sales speak different languages ​​lose money. The integrated model aligns everyone and maximizes results.

Key concepts:

Marketing-sales integration, revenue operations, unified model, organizational alignment, framework 8020.
