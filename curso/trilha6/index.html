Module 6.1

📋 How to set up a training program

Structure a training program from scratch, with diagnosis, objectives and practical calendar.

What is it:

Before teaching anything, you need to map out where each person is. Diagnose and assess current skills, knowledge gaps and behaviors that need to change. Without this, you train in the dark.

Why learn:

Generic training wastes time and demotivates. When you know exactly where each person fails, you can create surgical interventions that generate quick results.

Key concepts:

Skills assessment, gap analysis, baseline, skills mapping, individual vs collective diagnosis.

What is it:

Training objectives need to be specific and measurable. It's not "improving sales" - it's "being able to ask the 4 diagnostic questions in 100% of appointments by day X".

Why learn:

Without a clear objective, there is no way to know if the training worked. Well-defined objectives give direction to the coach and trainee.

Key concepts:

SMART objectives, learning indicators, behavioral goal, success criteria, alignment with business results.

What is it:

A training program doesn't have to be perfect to begin with. The minimum viable structure includes: theme of the day, guided practice and feedback. With these three elements, you already have a functional program.

Why learn:

Many companies never train because they think they need a "corporate university". In fact, 15 minutes a day with structure is a game changer.

Key concepts:

Training MVP, iteration, functional simplicity, lean training, continuous improvement.

What is it:

The calendar defines when each session takes place, who participates and what format will be used. Includes frequency (daily, weekly), duration and who is responsible for conducting each session.

Why learn:

Without a calendar, training becomes “whenever”. And when something becomes "when it happens", it never happens. The calendar creates commitment and rhythm.

Key concepts:

Session planning, optimal frequency, training routine, responsibility, cadence.

What is it:

Identify what you need: physical or virtual space, support materials, printed scripts, evaluation forms, audio/video recorder for simulations. Most cost zero.

Why learn:

Planning resources avoids improvisations that kill the program's credibility. It also shows that you don't need a big budget to train well.

Key concepts:

Resource inventory, support materials, training infrastructure, zero cost, essential tools.

Module 6.2
6.2 ~60 min

👥 Profile training

Customize training for each group: A (top performers), B (middle) and C (low performers).

What is it:

Group A has already mastered the basics. Training for them focuses on refinement: complex negotiations, upsell, cross-sell, mentoring others. They need challenges, not revision.

Why learn:

If you train Group A like you train Group C, they get bored and lose motivation. The best use of Group A is as a method multiplier.

Key concepts:

Mentoring, autonomy, advanced challenges, multiplier role, leadership development.

What is it:

Group B has potential but lacks consistency. The training focuses on: following the script, repeating until automated, correcting specific errors. They need structure and close monitoring.

Why learn:

Group B is where the highest return on investment is. With the right training, many from B migrate to A. They are the "middle of the funnel" of your team.

Key concepts:

Script as a guide, deliberate repetition, weekly monitoring, route correction, growth potential.

What is it:

Group C needs intensive intervention or repositioning. The training focuses on the absolute basics: posture, listening, initial questions. If it doesn't improve in 30 days, consider another role.

Why learn:

It's not about giving up on anyone - it's about being honest. Not everyone is born for sales. Give a fair chance with intensive training and respect the person and the business.

Key concepts:

Intensive intervention, evolution period, repositioning, alternative functions, data-based decision.

What is it:

Group A: fortnightly sessions, focus on mentoring. Group B: weekly sessions, focus on practice. Group C: daily sessions, focus on fundamentals. The format also changes: video, in-person, role play.

Why learn:

Applying the same load to everyone and giving the same medicine for different diseases. Customizing intensity maximizes results and minimizes waste.

Key concepts:

Training load, format per profile, differentiated frequency, personalization, training efficiency.

What is it:

Group A: evolution in 1-2 weeks. Group B: visible evolution in 30-60 days. Group C: definitive evaluation in 30 days. Setting realistic expectations avoids frustration on both sides.

Why learn:

Without a defined deadline, training becomes eternal. With a deadline, you create urgency and objective criteria to evaluate whether the investment in each person is paying off.

Key concepts:

Evolution deadline, realistic expectations, progress milestones, feedback assessment, decision-making based on data.

Module 6.3
6.3 ~75 min

📆 The 7-day method in practice

A day-by-day itinerary to train a salesperson from scratch in a full week.

What is it:

On the first day, the focus is on teaching the 4 diagnostic questions that every salesperson needs to master. These are questions that reveal the client's real situation, their pain and what they really need.

Why learn:

Questions are the basis of everything. A salesperson who asks questions is already 70% of the way there. Starting here ensures the foundation is solid before building the rest.

Key concepts:

Diagnostic questions, active listening, open vs closed questions, logical sequence, rapport.

What is it:

On the second day, the salesperson learns to identify pain (what the customer wants to avoid) and desire (what he wants to achieve). Every sale happens at the intersection of these two emotional engines.

Why learn:

Salespeople who talk about products lose to salespeople who talk about pain and desire. The customer doesn't buy what you sell - they buy the solution for what they feel.

Key concepts:

Customer pain, customer desire, purchase motivation, emotional triggers, emotional connection.

What is it:

On the third day, the salesperson learns to translate product characteristics into value for the customer. It's not "there's 10GB of space" - it's "you'll never lose an important file again".

Why learn:

Feature doesn't sell. Benefit sells. And benefits connected to customer pain sell even more. This skill is the bridge between diagnosis and proposal.

Key concepts:

Feature vs benefit, value proposition, tangible impact, result language, value translation.

What is it:

On the fourth day, the salesperson practices the complete service script: opening, diagnosis, value proposition, closing. The script is not meant to be robotic - it is a structure that ensures nothing is forgotten.

Why learn:

Salespeople without a script skip steps, forget questions and lose sales due to disorganization. The script is the track that keeps the train on track.

Key concepts:

Sales script, service script, sales steps, conversation flow, naturalization of the script.

What is it:

On the fifth day, the salesperson memorizes and practices the answers to the 5 most common objections: "it's expensive", "I'll think about it", "I need to talk to my partner", "I already have a supplier", "I don't need it now".

Why learn:

80% of sales are lost to predictable objections. If the seller already has the answer ready, he doesn't freeze and he doesn't improvise wrongly.

Key concepts:

Objections outline, ready answers, anticipation, real objections vs smoke screen, reformulation technique.

What is it:

On the sixth day, the seller carries out a complete service simulation recorded on video or audio. Then, watch with the trainer to identify areas for improvement.

Why learn:

No one improves what they don't see. The recording is a brutal but necessary mirror. The seller notices defects that he would never notice otherwise.

Key concepts:

Role play, simulation recording, self-assessment, guided review, constructive feedback.

What is it:

On the seventh day, the salesperson serves a real customer with the supervisor nearby. After the service, immediate feedback on what worked and what needs to be adjusted.

Why learn:

The transition from simulation to reality is the critical moment. With supervision, the seller has the confidence to apply the method without fear of making mistakes alone.

Key concepts:

Field supervision, immediate feedback, assisted service, simulation-reality transition, consolidation.

Module 6.4
6.4 ~60 min

🎭 Simulation and role play

Master the simulation technique as the main tool for developing salespeople.

What is it:

Simulation works because it activates the same neural networks as real care, but without the risk. The brain does not completely differentiate between training and reality, which is why athletes train before competing.

Why learn:

Understanding the science behind the simulation motivates the team to take training seriously. It's not "playing at selling" - it's neuromuscular training for sales.

Key concepts:

Neuroplasticity, deliberate practice, muscle memory, spaced repetition, active learning.

What is it:

Scenarios must be based on REAL situations the team faces. Use recorded appointments, frequent complaints, real objections. The more realistic, the more useful the training.

Why learn:

Generic scenarios generate generic training. Real scenarios prepare you for real situations. The team recognizes the scenario and gets involved because they know they will face it.

Key concepts:

Scenarios based on data, real situations, customer profiles, scenario bank, continuous updating.

What is it:

Three roles: seller (who trains), customer (who simulates real objections and behaviors) and observer (who notes points for improvement). Rotating roles enriches everyone’s learning.

Why learn:

Playing the role of the customer teaches empathy. Observing teaches critical analysis. Each role develops a different and complementary skill.

Key concepts:

Role rotation, role of the observer, empathy with the client, observation form, 360-degree learning.

What is it:

Use a checklist with objective criteria: did you ask the diagnostic questions? Did you identify the pain? Did it present value connected to pain? Did you get around the objection? Did you try closing? Each item is yes or no.

Why learn:

Subjective evaluation ("it was good") doesn't teach anything. Objective checklist shows exactly what was missing and what already works. Transform opinion into data.

Key concepts:

Assessment checklist, objective criteria, scoring, strengths and weaknesses, progress record.

What is it:

Effective feedback follows the formula: praise what worked, point out ONE thing to improve, show HOW to improve. Never more than one point of improvement at a time. And always end with encouragement.

Why learn:

Poorly given feedback destroys motivation and creates resistance to training. Well-given feedback accelerates evolution and builds trust. The difference is in the technique, not in the intention.

Key concepts:

Sandwich feedback, focus on behavior, one point at a time, immediate feedback, positive reinforcement.

Module 6.5
6.5 ~60 min

📈 Evolution metrics

Measure training progress with objective indicators and practical dashboards.

What is it:

The essential training indicators: conversion rate before/after, average ticket, number of diagnostic questions asked, objection bypass rate, post-service customer NPS.

Why learn:

Without metrics, training becomes an act of faith. With metrics, you prove ROI and justify continuing to invest. Data transforms opinion into fact.

Key concepts:

Training KPIs, before and after, process indicators, result indicators, correlation.

What is it:

Group A: expect 5-10% improvement on average ticket. Group B: expect 20-40% improvement in conversion. Group C: expect to reach the minimum baseline or not. Each group has different goals.

Why learn:

Comparing a C with an A is unfair and demotivating. Each profile evolves at a different pace and magnitude. Personalized goals are fair and motivating.

Key concepts:

Goals by profile, individual baseline, relative evolution, fair comparison, calibrated expectations.

What is it:

Signs that training works: salespeople use questions spontaneously, objections are no longer a problem, customers praise the service, numbers rise consistently.

Why learn:

Knowing how to read the signs of progress avoids abandoning a program that is working just because the results have not yet appeared in the final numbers.

Key concepts:

Leading indicators, qualitative signals, behavioral change, lag vs lead indicators, strategic patience.

What is it:

A simple visual dashboard that shows: seller name, group (A/B/C), metrics from the previous week, metrics from the current week, trend (rising/falling/stable). It could be a spreadsheet.

Why learn:

Visibility breeds responsibility. When everyone sees the numbers, the team regulates itself. The dashboard is the simplest and most powerful management tool there is.

Key concepts:

Operational dashboard, data visualization, transparency, trends, weekly monitoring.

What is it:

Triggers to adjust: metrics stagnated for 2 weeks, team unmotivated with the format, new product launched, change in customer profile, consistent feedback that something doesn't work.

Why learn:

A program that never changes becomes obsolete. One that changes too much never consolidates. Knowing the right adjustment triggers and balancing stability with adaptation.

Key concepts:

Adjustment triggers, program iteration, feedback loop, adaptation, continuous improvement.

Module 6.6
6.6 ~45 min

🔄 Continuous vs punctual training

Transform training from an isolated event into a permanent culture of development.

What is it:

The Ebbinghaus curve shows that we forget 70% of what we learn in 24 hours and 90% in 7 days, without review. One-off training generates temporary enthusiasm and zero results.

Why learn:

Many companies spend fortunes on one-day "trainings". The effect disappears within a week. Understanding neuroscience avoids this waste and redirects investment.

Key concepts:

Forgetting curve, retention, spaced review, memory consolidation, neuroscience of learning.

What is it:

10-15 minute sessions before work focused on a single topic: practicing an objection, reviewing a concept, sharing a success story. Short, focused, daily.

Why learn:

15 minutes per day = 5 hours per month of training. More than most companies invest in an entire year. And without taking anyone out of the operation.

Key concepts:

Microlearning, daily habit, focus on a theme, morning huddle, just-in-time learning.

What is it:

The ideal cadence combines: daily micro-training (15min), weekly role play session (30-45min), biweekly metrics review (30min), and monthly full assessment (60min).

Why learn:

Cadence creates rhythm. Rhythm creates habit. Habit creates results. The ideal cadence balances intensity with sustainability - not so much that you get tired, not so little that it doesn't change anything.

Key concepts:

Training cadence, sustainable pace, frequency layers, fixed calendar, collective commitment.

What is it:

Training culture and when the team WANTS to train, they don't need to be forced to. It happens when training is relevant, generates visible results and is conducted with respect for everyone's time.

Why learn:

Imposed training generates resistance. Desired training generates engagement. The difference is in showing results, not in demanding presence.

Key concepts:

Organizational culture, intrinsic motivation, perceived relevance, voluntary engagement, leadership by example.
