📋 Commercial Diagnostic Questionnaire

The 20 questions that reveal the real commercial profile of any salesperson. Tool ready to apply.

What is it:

A questionnaire structured into 4 blocks (aptitude, desire, learning and execution) with 5 questions each, totaling 20 questions that map the complete commercial profile of any professional.

Why learn:

Without diagnosis, you treat everyone the same. With the questionnaire, you know exactly where each salesperson needs to evolve and can create individual development plans.

Key concepts:

Commercial aptitude, desire to sell, learning capacity, execution discipline, Commercial Fit.

What is it:

Scoring system from 1 to 5 for each question. 1 = never/very weak, 2 = rarely/weak, 3 = sometimes/average, 4 = often/good, 5 = always/excellent. Total per block: 5 to 25 points.

Why learn:

The score transforms subjective perception into an objective number. It allows you to compare sellers, monitor progress and make decisions based on data.

Key concepts:

Likert scale, objective scoring, standardized assessment, baseline, performance measurement.

What is it:

Classification by block: 21-25 points = high competence, 14-20 points = medium competence, 5-13 points = low competence. Each block is evaluated separately to identify specific gaps.

Why learn:

It allows you to identify precisely where to invest in training. A salesperson with high aptitude but low execution needs monitoring, not a technical course.

Key concepts:

Competency ranges, gap analysis, diagnosis by dimension, development prioritization.

What is it:

The Commercial Fit is the average of the scores from the 4 blocks converted to a scale of 0-10. Score of 8.5-10 = strong, 7-8.4 = good, 5.5-6.9 = partial, below 5.5 = low commercial fit.

Why learn:

Commercial Fit is the only indicator that summarizes commercial capacity. Allows quick decisions about hiring, promotion, investment in training or relocation.

Key concepts:

Commercial Fit, general score, weighted average, profile classification, data-based decision.

What is it:

Complete form formatted and ready to print or use digitally. Includes instructions, scale, all 20 questions, space for scoring, automatic calculation and observation area.

Why learn:

Eliminates the “I don’t have a tool” excuse. The template is ready — just print and apply. Zero preparation time, 100% execution.

Key concepts:

Standardized template, evaluation form, management tool, immediate application.

🎙️ Ready-made Sales Script

Complete opening, diagnosis, value presentation and closing scripts ready to use.

What is it:

Opening script that creates genuine connection before any offer. Includes personal presentation model, context question and natural transition to the business conversation.

Why learn:

The first 30 seconds define whether the customer will listen to you or create a barrier. A good opening script eliminates initial resistance.

Key concepts:

Rapport, first impression, consultative opening, breaking resistance, context question.

What is it:

Sequence of 4 questions that reveal the customer's current situation, problem, impact and urgency. It flows like a natural conversation, not like an interrogation.

Why learn:

Without diagnosis, you present a generic solution. With diagnosis, you connect your offer to the customer's real problem and they feel understood.

Key concepts:

Situation questions, problem questions, impact questions, need questions, consultative selling.

What is it:

Presentation model that connects the diagnosis to the solution using the bridge phrase "From what you told me...". Personalize the proposal using the client’s own words.

Why learn:

When the customer hears their own words in the presentation, they feel that the solution was made for them. This dramatically increases the conversion rate.

Key concepts:

Bridge phrase, mirroring, personalized proposal, diagnosis-solution connection, perceived value.

What is it:

Techniques for leading the customer to make a decision without pressure. Includes closing questions, assumed alternative, and natural transition to next steps.

Why learn:

Many sellers do everything right until the end and lose the sale because they don't know how to close. The script eliminates this gap.

Key concepts:

Consultative closing, commitment question, alternative assumed, next steps, call to action.

What is it:

Adaptations of the base script for different sales contexts: services (intangible), products (tangible), B2B (companies) and B2C (end consumer). Each segment has its own language and approach.

Why learn:

A generic script does not work for everyone. Adaptations by segment ensure that the approach is relevant and natural for each type of customer.

Key concepts:

Segmentation, language adaptation, B2B vs B2C, selling services vs products, contextualization.

🛡️ Map of Objections and Responses

The 5 most common objections with 3 answer variations each. 15 ready-to-use answers.

What is it:

The price objection is the most common and usually hides another real objection. The 3 response variations address: lack of perceived value, comparison with competitors, and real budget limitations.

Why learn:

Salespeople who don't know how to answer "it's expensive" lose sales unnecessarily. With the 3 variations, you have an answer for any price context.

Key concepts:

Price objection, perceived value, anchoring, comparison, investment reformulation.

What is it:

When a client says “I need to think,” it usually means they lacked information, confidence, or urgency. The 3 variations treat each root cause differently.

Why learn:

"I need to think" is the most dangerous objection because it sounds polite but silently kills the sale. Those who leave to "think" rarely come back.

Key concepts:

Objection to postponement, lack of urgency, insufficient information, fear of deciding, rescue of interest.

What is it:

Variation of "I need to think" with an emphasis on timing. The 3 answers address: creating real urgency, highlighting the cost of waiting, and ensuring the next concrete step.

Why learn:

"After" never comes. If the seller does not address this objection right away, the timing will be lost and the sale will go cold. Every day of delay reduces the chance of closing.

Key concepts:

Urgency, cost of inaction, sales timing, concrete next step, structured follow-up.

What is it:

When the customer wants to compare, it may be due to insecurity, research habits or a lack of clear differentiation. The 3 answers help to position yourself before the comparison takes place.

Why learn:

If the customer goes out to compare without clear criteria, they only compare price. The answers help to define the comparison criteria in your favor.

Key concepts:

Differentiation, comparison criteria, positioning, value anchoring, competitive advantage.

What is it:

The priority objection reveals that the customer did not connect your solution to an urgent pain. The 3 answers help to highlight the impact of not resolving it now.

Why learn:

If the client says it is not a priority, the diagnosis has failed. Responses help reconnect the conversation to the real problem and create genuine urgency.

Key concepts:

Priority, pain vs desire, cost of inaction, problem reformulation, genuine urgency.

✅ Commercial Manager Checklist

Complete commercial manager routine: what to do per week, per month, what to observe and what to correct.

What is it:

List of activities that the commercial manager must perform every week: pipeline meeting, listening to calls, individual feedback, analyzing metrics and planning the following week.

Why learn:

Manager without a routine puts out fires. A manager with a weekly checklist prevents problems before they happen and keeps the team on pace.

Key concepts:

Management routine, pipeline meeting, one-on-one, weekly analysis, planning.

What is it:

Monthly review that includes: analysis of results vs goals, individual assessment of each salesperson, process review, strategy adjustment and planning for the following month.

Why learn:

The monthly cycle is where the manager makes strategic adjustments. Without this review, problems accumulate and only appear when it is too late.

Key concepts:

Monthly review, goals vs achieved, individual assessment, strategy adjustment, PDCA cycle.

What is it:

Behavioral indicators that reveal whether the salesperson is progressing or regressing: energy, volume of activities, quality of conversations, attitude in meetings, punctuality in follow-ups.

Why learn:

Numbers show the past. Behavior shows the future. The manager who observes early signs acts before results suffer.

Key concepts:

Behavior indicators, warning signs, leading indicators, active observation, early intervention.

What is it:

Hierarchy of correction priorities: 1) Volume of activity, 2) Quality of approach, 3) Pipeline management, 4) Closing technique. Always fix in the right order.

Why learn:

Correcting in the wrong order wastes time. There is no point teaching closing technique to those who do not do enough activity. Priority saves time and results.

Key concepts:

Prioritization, correction hierarchy, root cause, impact vs effort, quick wins.

What is it:

Model agenda with distribution of activities by day of the week. Includes blocks of time for meetings, analysis, feedback, training and planning.

Why learn:

Managers without a model agenda waste their day reacting. With the template, each day has a defined purpose and the team knows when to count on the leader.

Key concepts:

Time management, focus blocks, model agenda, management discipline, predictability.

📢 Content Plan by Level of Awareness

Complete content templates for each level of customer awareness: type, channel, frequency and examples.

What is it:

Content plan for people who don't yet know they have a problem. Focus on light entertainment and educational content that sparks curiosity without selling.

Why learn:

The largest share of the market is unconscious. If you only talk to those who already want to buy, you miss 80% of future opportunities.

Key concepts:

Top of the funnel content, awakening interest, market education, awareness, reach.

What is it:

Plan for those who already know they have a problem but don't know how to solve it. Content focused on naming the pain, amplifying consequences and showing that there is a way.

Why learn:

This audience is ready to be educated. The right content turns someone frustrated into someone actively looking for a solution — yours.

Key concepts:

Problem agitation, educational content, name of the pain, consequence of not acting, middle of the funnel.

What is it:

Plan for those who know they need something but don't know your product. Comparative content, demonstrations, success stories and social proof.

Why learn:

This public is evaluating options. The right content positions you as the best choice even before the sales conversation takes place.

Key concepts:

Social proof, comparison, differentiation, authority, consideration.

What is it:

Plan for those who already know your product but haven't purchased it yet. Offer content, guarantees, testimonials, demonstrations and risk reduction.

Why learn:

This audience is almost ready. The right content removes the last objections and creates the momentum for the purchasing decision.

Key concepts:

Irresistible offer, guarantee, testimonial, risk reduction, decision trigger.

What is it:

Plan for those who already want to buy and just need the final push. Urgent content, scarcity, bonuses, special conditions and direct call-to-action.

Why learn:

Not treating the very conscious differently and wasting money. He doesn't need more education — he needs ease in buying now.

Key concepts:

Real urgency, scarcity, direct CTA, bottom of the funnel, conversion, lightning offer.

📊 Commercial Performance Assessment

Complete evaluation system: indicators, monitoring, feedback and individual development plan.

What is it:

Specific metrics for each seller profile: hunters (prospecting volume), farmers (retention and upsell), closers (conversion rate), and SDRs (lead qualification).

Why learn:

Measuring everyone by the same metric is unfair and ineffective. Each profile has its own indicators that reveal real performance.

Key concepts:

KPIs by profile, activity metrics, result metrics, efficiency indicators.

What is it:

Structured template for monthly evaluation of each seller. Includes quantitative metrics, qualitative assessment, comparison with previous months and action plan.

Why learn:

Consistent monthly monitoring is what separates amateur management from professional management. Creates history, allows comparison and documents evolution.

Key concepts:

Periodic evaluation, performance history, trends, temporal comparison, recording.

What is it:

Feedback methodology that uses numbers and evidence instead of opinion. Structure: data + behavior + impact + suggestion. Removes subjectivity and defensiveness.

Why learn:

Feedback without data generates resistance. Feedback with data generates action. The seller cannot argue with his own numbers.

Key concepts:

Data-based feedback, SBI (Situation-Behavior-Impact), objectivity, action plan, accountability.

What is it:

PDI (Individual Development Plan) template with specific goals, deadlines, resources, people responsible and success indicators per salesperson.

Why learn:

Generic training doesn't work. Each seller has different gaps. PDI personalizes development and accelerates results.

Key concepts:

PDI, SMART goals, gap analysis, individual action plan, development monitoring.

What is it:

Consolidated visual dashboard with metrics for the entire team: ranking, trends, collective gaps, comparison between sellers and temporal evolution.

Why learn:

The dashboard allows a quick view of the team's commercial health. In 5 minutes the manager knows where the problem is and can act precisely.

Key concepts:

Dashboard, consolidated metrics, ranking, trend, management vision, quick decision making.
