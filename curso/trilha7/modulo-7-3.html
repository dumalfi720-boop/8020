🛡️ Map of Objections and Responses

The 5 most common objections with 3 answer variations each. 15 ready-made answers to use in any commercial situation.

Topic 1
1

💸 "It's expensive"

The price objection is almost never about price. And aboutperceived value. If the customer sees the value, price stops being a problem. Use the right variation depending on the real context of the objection.

Variation 1: Lack of perceived value

When the customer did not connect the price to the result

"I understand. Tell me one thing: if this [problem] you told me about continues for another 6 months, how much will it cost you? Because when we compare the investment with the cost of not solving it, the calculation changes a lot."

Logic: Repositions the price as an investment vs. the cost of inaction.

Variation 2: Comparison with competitor

When the customer has another price reference

"It makes sense to compare. I can ask you: when choosing, what is more important to you — the lowest price or what will really solve the problem? Because the cheap thing that doesn't work is expensive in the end."

Logic: Shifts the criterion from price to result. Position quality vs cost.

Variation 3: Real budget limitation

When it really doesn't fit in your pocket now

"I understand that the moment is tight. We can adjust the payment method or start with a leaner version. The important thing is that you don't waste any more [time/money/customers] waiting for the perfect moment. Which format would be more comfortable?"

Logic: Offers flexibility without giving discounts. Maintain a sense of urgency.

Topic 2
2

🤔 "I need to think"

"I need to think" and themost dangerous objectionbecause it seems polite but silently kills the sale. Those who leave to "think" rarely come back. The key is to understand what’s behind it.

Variation 1: Missing information

When the client does not have enough clarity to decide

"Of course, it's an important decision. Help me understand: what exactly do you need to evaluate better? That way I can give you the information you're missing now and you leave here with everything you need to decide."

Logic: Identifies the real doubt and resolves it immediately, preventing the sale from going cold.

Variation 2: Fear of deciding

When the customer is afraid of regretting

"I understand completely. Normally when someone tells me this, it's because they still have some concerns. Tell me: what would be the worst case scenario if you did it and didn't like it? Because we have [guarantee/support/trial period] exactly for that."

Logic: Reduces perceived risk. It shows that the decision is reversible.

Variation 3: Lack of urgency

When the customer does not feel they need to resolve it now

"No problem. Just one question: you told me that [problem] is costing you [impact]. If you wait another month, will this cost stop or continue? Because sometimes 'thinking' ends up costing more than 'acting'."

Logic: Uses the customer’s own words to create genuine urgency.

Topic 3
3

⏰ "I'll see later"

"After" never comes. If the seller accepts this objection passively,lose your timingand sales get colder with each passing day.

Variation 1: Create real urgency

"I understand that timing is important. I just want to be transparent: the [special condition / availability / current price] is valid until [date]. After that, [changes the price / closes the class / loses the spot]. I don't want you to miss this window."

Logic: Creates real urgency with a concrete deadline. Important: the urgency must be real.

Variation 2: Highlight the cost of waiting

"Can I ask you something? This problem you told me about — will it improve on its own over time or will it tend to get worse? Because if it's costing you [value] per month, each month that passes, more [value] is lost."

Logic: Shows that waiting has a cost. Inaction is not neutral — it is accumulated damage.

Variation 3: Ensure concrete next step

"Okay, I respect your time. But so I don't lose sight of you, can I schedule a quick call for [next date]? That way you can think calmly and we can get back to it without you needing to remember."

Logic: If it doesn't close now, at least it guarantees a concrete future commitment. Never accept "I'll call you".

Topic 4
4

⚖️ "I want to compare"

Comparing is natural, but if the client leaves without clear criteria,compare only by price. The strategy is to define the comparison criteria before it leaves.

Variation 1: Define criteria

"It makes perfect sense to compare. Can I help you with this? When evaluating, the 3 most important points to look at are: [criterion 1 where you are strong], [criterion 2 where you are strong], and [criterion 3]. This way you compare fairly and not just based on price."

Logic: You define the rules of the game. The customer will compare using YOUR criteria.

Variation 2: Anticipate differences

"Great, yes, compare. Just so you can have a reference: what differentiates us is [differential 1] and [differential 2]. Ask your competitor if they offer this. If so, great — if not, you already know what you're giving up."

Logic: Plants seeds of doubt about the competitor using their strengths.

Variation 3: Make the decision easier now

"I understand. But tell me: if everything you need is here — and from what we talked about, it is — what would be the reason for not solving it now? Sometimes comparison is more of a habit than a real need."

Logic: Questions whether the comparison is necessary or is it procrastination. Use with care — requires a good relationship.

Topic 5
5

🚫 "It's not a priority"

When the client says it is not a priority, it means thatthe diagnosis did not connect the pain sufficiently. The right answer reconnects the problem to real consequences.

Variation 1: Reveal the hidden cost

"I understand that you have a lot of things on your plate. Let me ask you: do you know how much this situation is costing you per month — in money, time or energy? Because sometimes what doesn't seem like a priority is precisely what is draining resources from your priorities."

Logic: Shows that the "non-priority" problem can be the root cause of other priority problems.

Variation 2: Connect with real priority

"It makes sense. And what is the main priority now? I ask because in many cases what we solve directly impacts what is a priority. For example, [example of connection between your solution and the customer's priority]."

Logic: Connects your solution to the customer's real priority instead of competing with it.

Variation 3: Respect and keep the door open

"I totally respect it. When do you think this can become a priority? I ask so I don't bother you too soon, but also so I'm ready when you need it. Can I send you some quick material via email and we'll get back to it within [deadline]?"

Logic: When the objection is real, respecting and maintaining the relationship is worth more than forcing a sale.

💡 Universal Principle of Objections

Every objection is adisguised question. "It's expensive" = "Convince me it's worth it". "I need to think" = "I'm not sure yet." "It's not a priority" = "I don't see how it helps me now." Never argue with the objection — answer the question behind it.
