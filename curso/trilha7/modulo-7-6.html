Commercial performance assessment

You can't improve what you don't measure. But measuring wrong is worse than not measuring at all — it generates wrong decisions. In this module: correct indicators per profile, ready-to-use templates and the complete evaluation and development model.

5 Topics ~45 min Practical Assessment
Topic 1
📏

1. Indicators by profile

The most common mistake: measuring everyone with the same ruler. The talented salesperson (Group A) needs different indicators than the trainable (Group B) and the resistant (Group C). Each profile has KPIs that make sense for their role and level.

Table of indicators by group

Indicator Group A (Talented) Group B (Trainable) Group C (Resistant)
conversion rate Main Evolution Not applicable
Average ticket Main Secondary Not applicable
Sales cycle Main Secondary Not applicable
Adherence to the script Secondary Main Secondary
Activity volume Secondary Main Secondary
Conversion evolution Monitor Main Monitor
Qualified leads Not applicable Secondary Main
Generated content Not applicable Not applicable Main
Group A: pure result

Mecca for what it delivers: how much it converts, how much it earns, how long it takes. Don't micromanage the process — give freedom and demand results.

Group B: evolution and process

Mecca for improvement: are you converting more than last month? Does it follow the script? Does it make the necessary volume? The path is as important as the destination.

Group C: indirect contribution

Meca for its contribution to the system: how many leads it qualified, how much content it generated, how it supported the operation. Support metrics, not closing.

📊

3. How to give data-driven feedback

No guesswork. Effective feedback starts with a number, compares it to a goal, and ends with a question and a suggestion. It is a process, not a trial. Here is the complete model.

4-Step Feedback Conversation Template

Step 1: Show the number

Start with fact, not opinion. Let the data speak.

"Maria, your conversion rate this month was 12%. Last month it was 18%."
Step 2: Compare with the goal

Contextualize. The number alone doesn't say anything — it needs a reference.

"Our target for your level is 20%. You are 8 points below, and down 6 points from last month."
Step 3: Ask what happened

Don't accuse — investigate. The seller almost always knows what happened. Let him talk.

"What do you think has changed? What happened differently this month in your conversations?"
Step 4: Suggest adjustment

Based on the diagnosis, offer concrete and measurable action.

"From what I'm seeing, the diagnosis has become shorter in your calls. How about this week we focus on using all 4 questions and we measure the impact next Friday?"

Feedback for guesswork

"I feel like you're not trying hard enough. You need to improve."
Result: defense, frustration, zero improvement

Data feedback

"Your conversion dropped from 18% to 12%. What changed? Let's analyze your calls together."
Result: diagnosis, direction, real improvement

Each seller needs their own development plan. There is no "generic training" that solves everything. The PDI (Individual Development Plan) is the map that guides each person's growth — reviewed every quarter.

Completed example: Group B seller

Seller: João Silva | Group B | 6 months in the role
Strengths: High volume of activity, good energy in calls, persistent in follow-up
To develop: Deeper diagnosis before presenting a price, dealing with the “it’s expensive” objection
Actions: (1) Practice 4 diagnostic questions until March 15 — Manager | (2) Weekly role play on price objection — João | (3) Review 2 recorded calls per week — both

When to review the PDI

Monthly
Quick check: are the actions being carried out? Need to adjust something?
Quarterly
Full review: did you meet your goals? New PDI for the next quarter.
If you change groups
If the salesperson evolves from B to A (or regresses), the PDI needs to be recalibrated.

Key metrics for the dashboard

▸ Total pipeline:summed value of all ongoing business. Shows the future health of the operation.
▸ Average conversion:% of leads that become customers. Benchmark: Between 15-25% is healthy for B2B.
▸ Revenue per seller:how much each one generates. Identifies who carries the team and who needs help.
▸ Hopper Speed:how long from lead to closing. The smaller it is, the more efficient the operation.
▸ Loss rate per stage:where business dies. Indicates specific bottlenecks in the process.
▸ Activity vs result:Those who work a lot and convert little need training, not more volume.
