Topic 1: SPIN Selling
1

🔄 SPIN Selling — Neil Rackham

Published in 1988, based on the largest research ever done on sales (35,000 calls analyzed in 12 years), SPIN Selling scientifically demonstrated thatquestions sell more than argumentsin complex sales. The SPIN method has revolutionized the way salespeople conduct discovery conversations.

Main Box

Central thesis

In large sales, aggressive closing techniques REDUCE the success rate. What works is a structured sequence of questions that makes the buyer articulate the need and value of the solution.

🔍
Situation
Current context
⚠️
Problem
Pains and difficulties
💥
Implication
Consequences
✨
Need
Value of the solution
How to apply

💡 How to apply it in practice

On your next discovery call, don’t start by presenting your product. Start with Situation questions to understand the context, progress to Problems, go deeper with Implications ("what happens if it doesn't resolve?") and finish with Need for a Solution ("what would be ideal?"). The customer will ask to see your product — you won't need to push.

Topic 2: Challenger Sale
2

⚔️ The Challenger Sale — Dixon & Adamson

CEB (now Gartner) research with thousands of salespeople revealed 5 salesperson profiles. THEChallenger— who teaches, adapts and takes control — outperforms all others, especially in complex sales. The "relationship" alone is not enough.

Central thesis

High-performing salespeople don't just respond to needs — they create needs. They teach the client something they didn't know, reposition the problem and guide the decision with constructive assertiveness.

  • ✓ Teach— Bring an insight that the customer didn’t have. Change the way he sees the problem.
  • ✓ Tailor— Adapt the message to the specific context of each stakeholder.
  • ✓ Take Control— Conduct the conversation about money and decisions without fear.

💡 How to apply it in practice

Before the next meeting, prepare an insight into the customer's market that they probably don't have. Start the conversation with "Most companies in your industry believe X, but the data shows Y..." — this positions you as a consultant, not a salesperson.

Topic 3: JOLT Effect
3

⚡ The JOLT Effect — Dixon & McKenna

After the Challenger Sale, the same team discovered an even bigger problem:40-60% of qualified deals die in “no decision”. The enemy is not the competitor or the "no" — it is the fear of making the wrong decision.

Central thesis

Indecision is not an objection. Objection is rational ("its price is high"). Indecision and emotional ("what if I choose wrong?"). Treating indecision as an objection makes the situation worse — more information creates more paralysis.

⚖️
Judge
Assess the level of indecision
🎯
Offer
Offer clear recommendation
🚧
Limit
Limit exploration
🛡️
Take risk off
Remove the risk

💡 How to apply it in practice

When the deal stagnates, stop sending more materials. Instead, say: "Based on what I've seen at similar companies, I recommend option X. Can I explain why?" Giving a clear recommendation reduces buyer anxiety. Limit options to 2-3, not 10.

Topic 4: Challenger Customer
4

🤝 The Challenger Customer — Adamson, Dixon et al

The Challenger Sale teaches you how to sell. Challenger Customer teachesfor those who sell within the organization. With 6.8 stakeholders on average involved in each B2B purchase, selling to the wrong person is the most expensive way to waste time.

Central thesis

Don't sell to those who agree with you (Talkers). Sell ​​to those who have the capacity to mobilize the organization (Mobilizers). The best contact is not the most friendly — it is the most internally influential.

✓ Mobilizers

  • Has internal credibility
  • Ask difficult questions
  • They may not be the friendliest
  • Move the organization to decide

✗ Talkers

  • They love talking to salespeople
  • Agree with everything you say
  • It has no real influence
  • Gives a false sense of progress

💡 How to apply it in practice

On your next deal, ask your contact: "When you last took an idea like this internally, how did the approval process work?" If the answer is vague, you are probably talking to a Talker, not a Mobilizer. Look for someone who has already made changes before.

Topic 5: Power of Value Selling
5

💎 The Power of Value Selling — Julie Thomas

Julie Thomas, CEO of ValueSelling Associates, shows thatselling value is not an abstract concept — it is a disciplined processof discovering, quantifying and communicating economic impact. The book transforms "selling value" from a slogan into a method.

Central thesis

Trust is built when the customer sees real numbers, not vague promises. The seller who quantifies value early in the process gains an irreversible competitive advantage — because no competitor can compete against the business case that you built together with the customer.

  • ✓ Discover the problem in depth (don't accept the first answer)
  • ✓ Quantify the impact in dollars, hours or percentage
  • ✓ Build the business case together with the client
  • ✓ Use Visionario Plan to align all parts

💡 How to apply it in practice

Create a "Value Plan" template with 4 columns: Problem, Financial Impact, Proposed Solution, Expected ROI. Fill it out together with the client in the second meeting. This turns the conversation from “do you want to buy?” to “let’s build the case together.”

Topic 6: Gap Selling
6

🎯 Gap Selling — Keenan

Keenan (yes, just a name) argues thatevery sale and about the gap between current state and future state. If there is no gap, there is no sale. If the gap is small, the price seems high. If the gap is huge, any price seems fair.

Gap as a sales engine

😰
Current State
Pain, inefficiency, loss
← THE GAP →
The bigger, the more urgent
🎯
Future State
Result, efficiency, gain

💡 How to apply it in practice

In every discovery, map 3 things: (1) How the customer operates today, with numbers. (2) How he would like to operate. (3) What the gap costs per month/year. When you show "this gap is costing R$50K/month", your R$10K/month product looks cheap.

Topic 7: Stop Selling and Start Leading
7

🧭 Stop Selling and Start Leading — Richardson

Duff Richardson, along with James Kouzes and Barry Posner (authors of the classic "The Leadership Challenge"), made a surprising discovery:The behaviors that make a leader followed are the same behaviors that make a salesperson chosen.

Central thesis

Buyers don't want to be "sold" — they want to be confidently led through a complex decision process. The salesperson who acts as a leader inspires confidence, creates vision for the future and removes uncertainty.

🗺️
Model the path
🔭
Inspires shared vision
🔄
Challenge the process
💪
Empowers others to act

💡 How to apply it in practice

In your next negotiation, instead of asking "what do you need to decide?", say "let me share how I would guide this process if I were in your shoes." Take leadership of the purchasing journey. The buyer wants someone who simplifies, not someone who waits for instructions.

Topic 8: Monetizing Innovation
8

💡 Monetizing Innovation — Ramanujam & Tacke

From Simon-Kucher & Partners, the largest pricing consultancy in the world, this book reveals that72% of product launches fail not because of technology, but because of pricing strategy. The solution? Think about price and value BEFORE building the product.

Central thesis

The conversation about price cannot happen at the end — it needs to happen at the beginning. Discover Willingness to Pay (WTP) before defining features. Build the product around the value the customer is willing to pay, not the other way around.

4 types of monetization failure:

1.Feature Shock — lots of features, little focus
2.Minivation — innovation too small
3.Hidden Gem — good product, poorly priced
4.Undead — nobody wants it, but nobody kills it

💡 How to apply it in practice

Before creating a new offering, interview 10 customers with one question: "If this solved [problem X], how much would you pay per month?" If the response is consistently greater than its cost, you have a viable product. If not, redesign before investing.

Topic 9: Confessions of the Pricing Man
9

💲 Confessions of the Pricing Man — Hermann Simon

Hermann Simon is the greatest pricing expert in the world. Founder of Simon-Kucher, he consulted more than 5,000 companies on price. Its main thesis:Most companies charge less than they should because they do not understand that price is an expression of value, not cost.

Central thesis

Price is not cost + margin. Price is the monetary expression of perceived value. A 1% increase in price generates, on average, an 11% increase in profit. No other lever has this impact.

✓ Value-Based Pricing

  • Pricing based on customer value
  • Margin proportional to impact
  • Price defense with conviction
  • Sustainable premium

✗ Cost-Plus Pricing

  • Price based on production cost
  • Arbitrary and thin margin
  • Easy discount, fragile price
  • Race to the bottom

💡 How to apply it in practice

Stop justifying price based on cost ("our team costs X, so we charge Y"). Start justifying the result: "Our service generates R$500K in savings per year. The investment is R$100K." When value exceeds price by 3-5x, the price objection disappears.

Topic 10: Negotiation Genius
10

🏆 Negotiation Genius — Malhotra & Bazerman

From Harvard Business School, this book teaches thatNegotiation is not war — it is value creation. The best negotiators expand the pie before dividing it. Framework applicable to both sales of R$10K and deals of R$10M.

Central thesis

Mediocre negotiators divide the existing pie. Genius negotiators create a bigger pie, then capture their share. The two most powerful tools: ZOPA (Zone of Possible Agreement) and BATNA (Best Alternative to Negotiated Agreement).

ZOPA — Zone of Possible Agreement

The price range at which both parties can close. Discovering ZOPA before trading gives you a strategic advantage.

BATNA — Best Alternative

What is your best alternative if this deal does not close? And what about the client? Whoever has a better BATNA negotiates with more power.

Value Creation

Before dividing, add variables: deadline, scope, exclusivity, payment. Each variable is an opportunity to create value for both.

💡 How to apply it in practice

Before negotiating price, map out: (1) What is my minimum price? (2) What is the maximum the customer would pay? (3) What variables besides price can I use? When the customer asks for a discount, instead of lowering the price, offer something more: "I can keep the price and add 3 extra months of support. Does it work?"
