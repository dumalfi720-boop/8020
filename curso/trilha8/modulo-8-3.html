🔀 B2B vs B2C sales: what changes

The structural differences between selling to businesses and selling to consumers — and the universal principles that apply in both worlds.

Topic 1
1

⏱️ Decision cycle

The most visible difference between B2B and B2C is in thetime and structure of the decision. In B2C, a person decides in minutes. In B2B, a committee decides in weeks or months. This changes EVERYTHING in the approach.

Direct comparison

🛒 B2C — Short and individual

  • → Decision in minutes to days
  • → 1 decision maker (the person himself)
  • → Emotion as the main driver
  • → Momentum and scarcity work
  • → Medium low-medium ticket
  • → High volume compensates for margin

🏢 B2B — Long and collective

  • → Decision in weeks to months
  • → 6-10 stakeholders involved
  • → Rationality + internal politics
  • → Business case and ROI are mandatory
  • → Medium high-very high ticket
  • → Each deal matters individually

📊 What does this change in practice

In B2C, you optimize the funnel toquick conversation. In B2B, you optimize forrelationship and deal progress. Trying to close a B2B deal with B2C urgency is the quickest way to lose credibility. Trying to build long-term relationships in B2C is a waste of resources.

Topic 2
2

🧩 Levels of awareness in B2B

In B2C, you deal with one level of awareness at a time (the buyer's). In B2B,each stakeholder can be at a different level. The user suffers from the problem daily. The CFO doesn't even know the problem exists. The CTO wants to know about the technology. You need to speak all languages.

Awareness map by stakeholder

😰

End user — Aware of the problem

Live the pain daily. Want an urgent solution. Speaks the language of functionality and usability. "I need something that does X faster."

🔧

Manager / CTO — Aware of the solution

You know there are options. Assess technically. It speaks the language of integration, security, scalability. "How does this connect to our stack?"

💼

CFO / CEO — Aware (or not) of the impact

Think about numbers, risk and priority. You may not even know the problem exists. Speaks the language of ROI, payback and opportunity cost. “How much does this impact the P&L?”

⚖️

Procurement / Legal — Aware of the process

Focuses on compliance, contract, SLA and risk. It may slow down or veto for procedural reasons. "What's the get-out clause?"

💡 Practical Exercise

For your next B2B deal, create a "message matrix": a table with each stakeholder, their level of awareness, their main concern and the key message you should take. A single presentation for all does not work.

Topic 3
3

📈 Building value in B2B

In B2C, value and oftenemotional and immediate: status, pleasure, convenience. In B2B, value needs to bequantified in financial impact: ROI, risk reduction, efficiency gain. Without a business case, there is no budget approval.

The 4 pillars of value in B2B

💰
Direct ROI
"Generates R$X in additional revenue" or "Saves R$Y per month"
⏱️
Operational efficiency
"Reduces process time from X hours to Y minutes"
🛡️
Risk reduction
"Eliminates compliance risk" or "Reduces downtime by X%"
📊
Competitive advantage
"Puts you X months ahead of the competition"

💡 The business case rule

In B2B, if you can't fill out this sentence, you're not ready to sell: "Our solution solves [specific problem] that costs [quantified R$] per [period], generating [measurable result] in [deadline]. The investment pays for itself in [X months]." Practice until it feels natural.

Topic 4
4

🎤 Script and objections in B2B

In B2C, objections are typically personal: "it's expensive", "I don't need it", "I'll think about it". In B2B, objections areorganizational and systemic: involve budget, timing, internal processes and corporate policy. The script needs to navigate layers of complexity.

B2B vs B2C objections

B2C: "It's expensive"

Answer: anchoring in emotional benefit, installments, scarcity.

B2B: "Not in the budget"

Answer: "What is the cost of NOT solving it? Can we structure it for the next budget cycle? Is there another department's budget that this impacts?"

B2C: "I'll think about it"

Answer: create urgency, limited supply, quick follow-up.

B2B: "I need to approve internally"

Answer: "Can I help prepare the material for the internal presentation? Who else needs to be involved? What is the approval process?"

B2C: "I don't need it"

Answer: reveal latent pain, show the consequences of not acting.

B2B: "We have a contract with another supplier"

Answer: "When does the contract expire? What would make you consider alternatives? Can I show you a comparison of value?"

💡 Practical Exercise

Map out the 5 most common objections you hear. For each, identify: (1) Is it a value, timing, process or policy objection? (2) Who is really behind this objection? (3) What is the answer that RESOLVES, not just rebuts? The goal is not to win the argument — it is to remove the obstacle.

Topic 5
5

🔗 What applies the same

Despite all the differences,The fundamental principles are the same. People buy from those they trust. Value precedes price. Emotion influences every decision — even in B2B. The fundamentals of 8020 Tracks 1-7 apply in any context.

Universal sales principles

  • ✓ Trust as a basis— No one buys from someone they don't trust, whether it's a consumer buying a cell phone or a CFO approving a R$1M project.
  • ✓ Value before price— Order always matters: first the customer understands what they get, then they discuss what they pay. This goes for a course costing R$97 or a SaaS costing R$500K/year.
  • ✓ Active listening— The best salesperson is the one who listens the most, not the one who talks the most. In B2C or B2B, discovery is king.
  • ✓ Diagnosis before prescription— Don’t present a solution before understanding the problem. This goes for a doctor, a B2C salesperson and an AE enterprise.
  • ✓ Disciplined follow-up— Most sales happen after the 5th contact. Whoever gives up on the 2nd loses money, in any market.
  • ✓ 80/20 principle— 20% of customers generate 80% of revenue. 20% of activities generate 80% of results. This is universal.

📊 The big conclusion

B2B and B2C aredifferent contexts, not different planets. The principles are the same — the application changes. A salesperson who masters the fundamentals can move between both worlds. What changes is the complexity of execution, not the essence of the work.

What you learned:
